100,000 subscribers. 5 years in prison.

Augustinos Samaan is a Coptic Christian scholar with 100,000 subscribers. Egypt just sentenced him to prison for "misuse of social media." The Bible has a different verdict.

Persecution 2 Timothy 3:12 Matthew 5:10-12
Read below ↓

Augustinos Samaan is a Coptic Christian scholar. He runs a YouTube channel with more than 100,000 subscribers. He teaches the Bible, defends the faith, and answers questions about Christianity in a nation where Christians have lived as second-class citizens for fourteen centuries. On May 1, an Egyptian court sentenced him to five years in prison and hard labor. The charge was Article 98(f) of Egypt's penal code: blasphemy. The real crime was teaching Christianity online.

Egypt's constitution guarantees freedom of religion. Its blasphemy laws guarantee the opposite. Since August 2025, dozens have been arrested for online religious content. Converts. Apologists. Ordinary people who answered a question about Jesus on the internet. The pattern is not accidental. It is structural. The state permits Christianity as a private hobby. It punishes Christianity as a public claim.

This is not a human rights story. It is a faithfulness story.

The promise of persecution

Paul does not say some will suffer. He does not say the unlucky will suffer. He says all who desire to live godly in Christ Jesus will suffer persecution (2 Timothy 3:12). The promise of the Christian life is not exemption from suffering. It is inclusion in it. When Jesus called disciples, He did not promise comfort. He promised a cross. The Egyptian court handed Samaan five years. Christ handed His people something far more severe, and far more precious: the honor of sharing in His sufferings.

The Beatitudes turn the world's logic inside out. The poor in spirit are blessed. The mourners are comforted. And the persecuted — specifically for righteousness' sake — receive the kingdom of heaven. "Blessed are you when they revile and persecute you, and say all kinds of evil against you falsely for My sake. Rejoice and be exceedingly glad, for great is your reward in heaven" (Matthew 5:10-12). Rejoice and be exceedingly glad. Not "seek legal recourse." Not "start a petition." Rejoice. The Egyptian judge thought he was issuing a sentence. God calls it a beatitude.

The chain that cannot hold the Word

Peter and John faced the same arithmetic. The Sanhedrin arrested them, threatened them, and commanded them to stop speaking in the name of Jesus. Their response was not a compromise. It was a collision. "Whether it is right in the sight of God to listen to you rather than God, you must judge. For we cannot but speak the things which we have seen and heard" (Acts 4:19-20).

The early church did not calculate risk-benefit ratios. They calculated obedience. Samaan could have taught something else. He could have muted his convictions. He could have kept his Christianity offline, polite, and invisible — the way Egypt prefers it. He did not. And for that, he is not a cautionary tale. He is a confirmation of what the church has always known: the Word of God is not chained (2 Timothy 2:9).

What the martyrs knew

John Knox, facing the galleys and the scaffold, prayed: "Give me Scotland, or I die." He did not negotiate with Mary Queen of Scots. He preached. Richard Wurmbrand, tortured for fourteen years in Communist Romania, wrote that "suffering is the badge of the true church." He did not seek religious liberty. He sought the liberty of God to be God. John Calvin, writing to persecuted believers in France, reminded them that Christ reigns from heaven, and no earthly prison can silence the voice of the King.

They understood what Western Christians are slowly learning: persecution is not an interruption of the church's mission. It is the mission. Jesus sent His apostles as sheep in the midst of wolves. He did not send them as wolves in the midst of sheep. The church grows not by conquest but by confession — and confession, in a fallen world, provokes opposition.

The hand that holds the prison key

So what do we do with Augustinos Samaan? We do not pity him. We pray for him. We do not treat his imprisonment as a tragedy to be escaped but as a trial to be shared. We remember that the same hand that holds the galaxies holds the Egyptian prison key. The same God who numbered the hairs on Samaan's head numbered the days of his sentence before a single video was uploaded. This is not fatalism. It is providence.

And we do the thing he cannot do from a prison cell: we keep speaking. We keep uploading. We keep preaching. We keep confessing that Jesus is Lord in a world that would rather jail us than hear us. Because the Word of God is not chained. Not in Watford. Not in Cairo. Not in Rome. Not in Jerusalem. And certainly not on the internet.

The prison cell is not a defeat. It is a confirmation. Preach anyway.
