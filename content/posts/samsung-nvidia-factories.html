Samsung announced a $1 trillion investment in South Korean chip manufacturing this week — a number so large it breaks the normal grammar of corporate press releases. Nvidia and Corning announced a separate $2.7 billion factory for advanced glass substrates in the United States. The financial press used the words "historic," "transformative," and "era-defining." The national security establishment cited "supply chain resilience." The workers who will spend 12-hour shifts in clean room suits, breathing filtered air and handling silicon wafers under yellow light, were not quoted in any of the announcements. They never are.

$1 trillion is a staggering number. It is not a saving number. The semiconductor fab is a marvel of organized human intelligence. It is not a source of meaning, and meaning is what the press releases are accidentally selling. The language around these factories is a liturgy — "historic investment," "defining the era," "building the future" — and liturgies do something to the people who recite them whether they know it or not. The church should be able to spot a liturgy when it's being performed, even when the incense is disguised as market analysis.

The trillion-dollar bet

Samsung's crown is real. It is also leased. "Riches are not forever, nor does a crown endure to all generations" (Proverbs 27:24). The semiconductor market is cyclical. The geopolitical advantage that justifies the factory's location can vanish in a single election. Every manufacturing lead is temporary. Every market position is a bet, not a destiny. Samsung was not always the dominant player, and it won't always be. The $1 trillion commitment is not a prophecy. It is a wager placed in public, and the public is invited to treat it as inevitability. Paul's charge to Timothy is a standing order for every boom cycle: "As for the rich in this present age, charge them not to be haughty, nor to set their hopes on the uncertainty of riches, but on God, who richly provides us with everything to enjoy" (1 Timothy 6:17). $1 trillion is uncertain. Everything the financial press called permanent this week is provisional, and the only certain riches are the ones stored where "neither moth nor rust destroys."

The clean room worker in the Samsung fab and the executive who announced the investment from Seoul are breathing the same air, but they are living in different economies. The executive sees legacy — market share, national prestige, his name on the release. The worker sees a paycheck, and the paycheck may not reflect what his labor is worth. The trillion dollars does not answer the question both men are asking with their lives, whether they know it or not. The trillion dollars is an abstraction so large it ceases to mean anything at the scale of a single human life. That is not an accident. It is how the system scales.

Who holds the silicon together

Paul's Christ hymn in Colossians makes the ultimate claim on every factory, every wafer, every supply chain: "For by Him all things were created, in heaven and on earth, visible and invisible, whether thrones or dominions or rulers or authorities — all things were created through Him and for Him. And He is before all things, and in Him all things hold together" (Colossians 1:16–17). The silicon wafer holds together at the atomic level because Christ sustains it there. The supply chain functions not because of logistics contracts but because the Logos who ordered the universe continues to order it. If He withdrew His hand, the clean room, the fab, the glass substrate factory, the semiconductor market — all of it would collapse not into recession but into non-being. $1 trillion is an impressive number. It is not a sustaining number. Only one number holds the universe in existence, and Samsung's accountants cannot find it on the balance sheet.

$1 trillion is an impressive number. It is not a sustaining one. The silicon holds together because Christ holds it together — and no supply chain survives the withdrawal of the Logos.
