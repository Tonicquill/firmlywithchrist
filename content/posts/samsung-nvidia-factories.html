Samsung announced a $1 trillion investment in South Korean chip manufacturing this week — a number so large it breaks the normal grammar of corporate announcements. Separately, Nvidia and Corning announced a $2.7 billion joint factory for advanced glass substrates in the United States. The financial press used the words "historic," "transformative," and "era-defining." The national security establishment used the words "supply chain resilience" and "strategic independence." The workers who will spend 12-hour shifts in clean room suits, breathing filtered air and handling silicon wafers under yellow light, were not quoted in any of the press releases.

$1 trillion is an impressive number. It is not a saving number. The semiconductor fab is a marvel of human engineering. It is not a source of ultimate meaning. The church should be able to say both things without sounding like it is against technology, prosperity, or jobs. The factories are real achievements. The language around them is a liturgy, and the church should recognize liturgies when it sees them.

Uncertain riches and the living God

Paul's charge to Timothy is a standing order for every economic boom cycle: "As for the rich in this present age, charge them not to be haughty, nor to set their hopes on the uncertainty of riches, but on God, who richly provides us with everything to enjoy" (1 Timothy 6:17). $1 trillion is uncertain. The semiconductor market is cyclical. The geopolitical advantage that motivates the factory's location can shift in a single election cycle. Every manufacturing lead is temporary. Every market dominance is provisional. The only certain riches are the ones stored where "neither moth nor rust destroys" (Matthew 6:19–20).

Proverbs delivers the same verdict in eight words: "Riches are not forever, nor does a crown endure to all generations" (Proverbs 27:24). Samsung's crown is real. It is also temporary. Samsung was not always the market leader, and it will not always be the market leader. The $1 trillion commitment is a bet, not a prophecy. The bet may pay off. The bet may fail. Either way, the living God is the only foundation that cannot shift.

The eye that is never satisfied

The Preacher's scalpel cuts through the earnings report: "There is one alone, without companion: he has neither son nor brother. Yet there is no end to all his labors, nor is his eye satisfied with riches. But he never asks, 'For whom do I toil and deprive myself of good?' This also is vanity and a grave misfortune" (Ecclesiastes 4:8). The market can expand forever and the soul can still be hollow. The worker in the clean room suit can build the most advanced chips on earth and never ask the question the Preacher says is the only one that matters: who is this for?

The gap between what these factories mean to the executive announcing them and the worker staffing them is a theological gap before it is an economic one. The executive sees a legacy — market share, national prestige, his name on the press release. The worker sees a paycheck. The gap is not incidental to the system. It is the system. Cotton Mather warned the Massachusetts legislature in 1709 that abstraction in finance made divine providence harder to read. The $1 trillion number is an abstraction so large it ceases to mean anything at the level of a human life. That is not an accident of scale. It is a feature of the kind of economy we have built, and the church should name it.

The wages that cry out

James gives the clean room worker a voice the financial press will not: "Behold, the wages of the laborers who mowed your fields, which you kept back by fraud, are crying out against you, and the cries of the reapers have reached the ears of the Lord of Sabaoth" (James 5:4). Is Samsung paying its fab workers what their labor is worth, or is the $1 trillion number a function of suppressing labor costs while celebrating capital investment? The question is not rhetorical. It is judicial, and it will be answered in a courtroom where earnings reports are not admissible as evidence for the defense.

Hannah's prayer names the real economic agent behind every trillion: "The LORD makes poor and makes rich; He brings low and lifts up" (1 Samuel 2:7). Not the market. Not Samsung. Not Nvidia. The LORD. The silicon wafer in the clean room was His before it was Samsung's. The rare earth metals in the chip were His before they were mined. The sand that becomes glass substrate belongs to Him before Corning touches it. "The earth is the LORD's and the fullness thereof" (Psalm 24:1) is not a metaphor. It is a property claim, and the titleholder does not negotiate with tenants.

In Him all things hold together

Paul's Christ hymn in Colossians makes the ultimate claim on every factory, every chip, every supply chain: "For by Him all things were created, in heaven and on earth, visible and invisible, whether thrones or dominions or rulers or authorities — all things were created through Him and for Him. And He is before all things, and in Him all things hold together" (Colossians 1:16–17). The silicon holds together because Christ holds it together. The supply chain functions because Christ sustains it. If He withdrew His hand, the clean room, the fab, the glass substrate, the semiconductor market — all of it would collapse, not into recession but into non-being. $1 trillion is an impressive number. It is not a sustaining number. Only one number holds the universe in existence, and it cannot be printed on an earnings report.

$1 trillion is an impressive number. It is not a saving number. The silicon holds together because Christ holds it together — and if He let go, no supply chain could stop the collapse.
