The ceasefire was announced on May 3. Diplomats called it "the end of hostilities." The UN Security Council issued a statement praising "restraint." The financial press marked the occasion with a relief rally — oil futures down, airline stocks up, risk-on. Operation Epic Fury, the US-led bombing campaign that killed at least 2,362 Iranian civilians including 217 children, is formally over. The word "peace" has been deployed. But nothing is over.

The rubble in Tehran has not been cleared. The elementary school hit by a missile has not been rebuilt. The parents of the 217 dead children are still parents of dead children. A ceasefire is a legal instrument. It is not a resurrection. The prophet Jeremiah issued the standing warning against those who announce peace where there is none: "They have healed the wound of my people lightly, saying, 'Peace, peace,' when there is no peace" (Jeremiah 6:14). The wound is not healed. The bandage is a press release.

The state pronounces peace. The body counts the dead.

Jeremiah's indictment was directed at prophets who served the state's narrative rather than God's truth. The dynamic has not changed in 2,600 years. When diplomats say "peace," they mean "the bombing has stopped." When the grieving say "peace," they mean "my child is alive." Those are not the same word. They are not even in the same language. The ceasefire is real and the ceasefire is a lie, and both things are true at once. The church's job is not to choose which truth to tell. It is to hold both — to acknowledge that the bombs have stopped and to refuse to call that peace when the dead are still dead.

The Reformed doctrine of total depravity is relevant here in a specific way. It does not mean humans are as bad as they could possibly be. It means sin affects every part of human nature, including political reasoning. States make war for mixed motives — some legitimate, some criminal — and states make peace for mixed motives likewise. The ceasefire is not pure. The war was not pure. The only pure actor in this drama is the one who "neither slumbers nor sleeps" (Psalm 121:3) while diplomats and generals clock out.

The LORD kills and makes alive

Hannah's prayer in 1 Samuel 2 is the most politically incorrect text in Scripture and the most necessary: "The LORD kills and makes alive; He brings down to the grave and brings up" (1 Samuel 2:6). This is not a claim that God authorized the specific bombing of an elementary school. It is a claim that no bomb falls outside His governance, and no death escapes His jurisdiction. The child killed in Tehran did not die outside the will of God, and neither will the general who ordered the strike escape the judgment of God.

Hannah continues: "The LORD makes poor and makes rich; He brings low and lifts up" (1 Samuel 2:7). The ceasefire does not determine who is poor and who is rich. God does. The reconstruction contracts do not determine who is lifted up and who is brought low. God does. The state's instruments — war, ceasefire, sanctions, aid packages — are real but secondary. The primary cause of every outcome is the will of the Creator. That is either terrifying or comforting, depending on whether you trust Him.

The adversaries will be broken

Hannah's prayer closes on a note the diplomatic corps will never include in a readout: "The adversaries of the LORD shall be broken to pieces; against them He will thunder in heaven" (1 Samuel 2:10). Every participant in this war and its ceasefire — every pilot, every general, every diplomat, every president and supreme leader — will stand before a Judge whose jurisdiction has no sunset clause. There is no statute of limitations in the divine courtroom. The ceasefire does not cancel the court date.

The church does not celebrate ceasefires. The church waits for the return of the King, and in the meantime it buries its dead, prays the Psalter, and refuses to call anything "peace" that is merely the absence of active bombing. Real peace is not the cessation of war. Real peace is the restoration of all things, and that has not happened yet.

A ceasefire is a legal instrument. It is not a resurrection. The bombs have stopped. The dead are still dead. "Peace, peace," they say — when there is no peace.
