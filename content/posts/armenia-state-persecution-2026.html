Armenia was the first nation to adopt Christianity as its state religion. That was AD 301. In 2026, its government is criminalizing the same faith — banning travel for church leaders, tightening a surveillance apparatus around congregations, and telling pastors what they may and may not preach.

The Pashinyan government frames its campaign as "national security." It's an old play. When the state decides the church is a political threat, the state rebrands persecution as public safety. The Armenian Apostolic Church leadership has called the legislation a direct assault on religious freedom. The international community has called it nothing at all.

The LORD is our Judge. The LORD is our Lawgiver. The LORD is our King.

Isaiah 33:22 presents the Triune God in three offices that no Prime Minister, no parliament, and no security apparatus can occupy: Judge, Lawgiver, King. The verse is a declaration of exclusive divine jurisdiction. When Pashinyan's government demands that the Armenian church submit its preaching to state review, it is not merely overreaching politically. It is attempting to occupy a throne that belongs to Christ alone.

The Reformers died for this distinction. Zurich, Geneva, Westminster — every confessional tradition insists that "God alone is Lord of the conscience" (WCF 20.2). No civil magistrate may "assume to himself the administration of the Word and sacraments" (WCF 23.3). What is happening in Yerevan is not a policy dispute. It is a jurisdictional war.

Christ is the only Head of His Church

The travel bans are targeted. Armenian church leaders cannot leave the country to attend ecumenical meetings, to visit their diaspora, to speak at conferences. The message is explicit: the state controls where Christ's under-shepherds may go. But the Head of the Church does not need a visa. He does not present His passport at the airport. He is not subject to any travel restriction issued by any government in any nation at any point in history.

"He is the head of the body, the church" (Colossians 1:18). Not Pashinyan. Not the National Assembly. Not the security services. Christ. If the Armenian church obeys the travel ban, it is obeying a rival head. There is no neutral ground here.

We must obey God rather than men

The apostolic rule for this situation was established in Jerusalem, before the Sanhedrin, when Peter and the other apostles delivered the irreducible principle of Christian political theology: "We must obey God rather than men" (Acts 5:29). Not "we must negotiate with men." Not "we must seek a reasonable accommodation with men." Obey. God. Rather. Than. Men.

The Armenian church leaders who refuse to register their sermons with the state are not rebels. They are faithful. The pastors who travel despite the ban are not criminals. They are obedient to a higher jurisdiction.

The Judge will break every oppressor

Isaiah 33 closes with a vision of Jerusalem at peace under her true King. But the chapter does not get there without judgment. "The peoples will be burned to lime, like cut thorns that are burned in the fire" (Isaiah 33:12). The imagery is agricultural — thorns are worthless, cut thorns exist only to be burned. Every government that uses its power to suppress the witness of Christ will face the same disposal.

Armenia's government is not eternal. Pashinyan's tenure is not indefinite. And the Judge who sees every travel ban, every intercepted sermon, every state demand for theological submission — He is not indifferent. He is patient. That is not the same thing.
