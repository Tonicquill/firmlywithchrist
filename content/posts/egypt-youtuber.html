Augustinos Samaan is a Coptic Christian scholar with a YouTube channel of more than 100,000 subscribers. He teaches the Bible, answers questions about Christianity, and defends the faith in Arabic — a language spoken by four hundred million people, most of them in nations where leaving Islam is a criminal act. His videos are conversational: a man in a study, a Bible open on the desk, answering questions from viewers in Egypt, Sudan, and the diaspora. On May 1, an Egyptian court sentenced him to five years in prison with hard labor. The charge was Article 98(f) of Egypt's penal code: blasphemy. The prosecution cited his online videos as evidence of contempt for Islam.

Egypt's 2014 constitution guarantees freedom of religion. Its blasphemy laws guarantee the opposite. Since August 2025, Egyptian courts have handed down prison terms to dozens of citizens for online religious content: a convert who posted a video about his baptism, an apologist who debated a Muslim scholar on a livestream, a woman who shared a Bible verse on Facebook. The same logic was applied in Watford in April, where a pastor was arrested for street preaching under a public order statute. The pattern is structural, not accidental. The state permits Christianity as a private hobby — worship in a registered building, prayer in a home. It punishes Christianity as a public claim — a video accessible to anyone with an internet connection.

The courtroom where Samaan was sentenced looks like any other administrative chamber in Cairo: fluorescent lights, wooden benches, a judge in black robes reading from a file. The difference is that the defendant is not accused of fraud or theft. He is accused of explaining the Trinity to a camera. The sentence — five years of hard labor — means quarry work, construction detail, or textile manufacturing for a man in his fifties whose only crime was teaching. His channel remains online. The videos are still playing. The state could not shut them down, so it shut him down instead.

This is not new. In 1960s Romania, Richard Wurmbrand, a Lutheran pastor, was arrested for teaching Christianity in a communist state that also claimed to guarantee religious freedom. He spent fourteen years in prison, three of them in solitary underground. He wrote later that the church does not need freedom to be faithful; it needs faith to be free. Wurmbrand and Samaan are separated by sixty years and a continent, but they share the same arithmetic: a state that promises freedom while criminalizing witness is not a secular neutral. It is a state that has misunderstood the limits of its own authority.

The Reformed tradition has a specific name for this misunderstanding. The Westminster Confession of Faith calls the civil magistrate "God's minister" for good, not for evil. When a magistrate punishes what God commands — the open testimony of Christ — the magistrate has not issued law. He has issued a usurpation. The Coptic church has lived under this kind of pressure for fourteen centuries. Samaan's sentence is the latest chapter in a much older book.

Blessed are those who are persecuted for righteousness' sake, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven. Blessed are you when they revile and persecute you, and say all kinds of evil against you falsely for My sake. Rejoice and be exceedingly glad, for great is your reward in heaven.

Matthew 5:10-12

The Beatitudes do not say "blessed are the comfortable." They say blessed are the persecuted — specifically for righteousness' sake. The Egyptian judge thought he was issuing a sentence. Jesus calls it a beatitude. This is not a reinterpretation. It is a reclassification of the same facts under a different authority.

Peter and John faced the same arithmetic before the Sanhedrin. Ordered to stop speaking in Jesus' name, they answered that they could not but speak what they had seen and heard Acts 4:19-20. The early church did not calculate risk-benefit ratios. They calculated obedience. Samaan could have muted his convictions. He kept teaching. That choice is the central act of his faith, not an incidental consequence of it.

The Egyptian court can imprison a body. It cannot imprison a voice it did not create. That distinction — between what the state can reach and what it cannot — is the oldest conviction in the Christian faith, and the one Samaan is now testing in a Cairo prison cell.
