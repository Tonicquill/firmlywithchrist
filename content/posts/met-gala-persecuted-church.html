The Met Gala raised $42 million on Monday night. Celebrities ascended the museum steps in outfits costing more than most Americans earn in a year. The theme was "Radical Forms: Fashion and the Avant-Garde." The press called it dazzling, iconic, a triumph. The photographs traveled around the world in seconds.

The same week, in Rajasthan, Catholic worshippers were arrested after a mob armed with knives stormed their Sunday Mass. In Odisha, a fact-finding team documented beatings, sexual assault, and police complicity — 18 years after the pogrom that killed roughly a hundred Christians. Neither story had a step-and-repeat. Neither raised $42 million. The persecuted church does not have a dress code, Anna Wintour, or a red carpet. It has the blood of the Lamb and the word of its testimony, and in the economy of the kingdom, those are worth more than the entire Costume Institute endowment. That is not sentiment. It is eschatological math.

James speaks to this tableau with a directness the church has spent two millennia trying to soften. He does not critique. He pronounces doom: "Come now, you rich, weep and howl for the miseries that are coming upon you. Your riches have rotted and your garments are moth-eaten. Your gold and silver have corroded, and their corrosion will be evidence against you and will eat your flesh like fire" (James 5:1–3). James is not being dramatic. He is being eschatological. The corrosion of the gold is not a literary device. It is a witness for the prosecution. Every dollar spent on a dress that will be worn once, photographed, and archived is a dollar that will enter the divine court record as evidence of what its owner valued.

The prophet Amos preached to the ruling class of the northern kingdom — prosperous, secure, culturally sophisticated — and delivered the most savage class analysis in Scripture without using a single abstract noun: "Woe to those who lie on beds of ivory and stretch themselves out on their couches... who drink wine in bowls and anoint themselves with the finest oils — but are not grieved over the ruin of Joseph" (Amos 6:4–6). The sin is not the ivory beds. The sin is not the wine. The sin is the indifference. The Met Gala is ivory and wine and oil and music rendered in couture, and the ruin of Joseph — the arrested Catholics in Rajasthan, the assaulted women in Odisha, the church beaten with iron rods for the crime of existing — is not on the seating chart. No one at the Met Gala raised a glass for the persecuted. No one at the Met Gala was asked to.

The Beatitudes invert the Met Gala's entire value system in eight words: "Blessed are those who are persecuted for righteousness' sake, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven" (Matthew 5:10). Not the rich. Not the famous. Not the exquisitely dressed. The hunted. The Greek is dediogmenoi — pursued, harassed, driven out. The Catholics who fled the knife-wielding mob in Rajasthan. The believers in Odisha who sleep in the jungle because their homes were torched. The kingdom belongs to them. The Met Gala raised $42 million. The persecuted church owns the kingdom. The exchange rate is not close. It is not even measurable in the same currency.

$42 million for one night of costumes. The persecuted church owns the kingdom. Those are not comparable line items. One runs on the dollar. The other runs on the blood of the Lamb.
