Anthropic released Mythos this week. The tech press reached for its thesaurus of awe: "frontier," "superhuman," "era-defining." Within 48 hours, the awe curdled into terror — the same cycle that accompanied every major model drop since GPT-4. The liturgy is fixed now: release, marvel, panic, normalize, repeat. Silicon Valley keeps manufacturing gods and then trembling before them. The cycle is not about the technology. It is about the worship.

The pattern is older than the transistor. It is older than the printing press. Romans diagnosed it in the first century: "They exchanged the truth of God for a lie and worshiped and served the creature rather than the Creator" (Romans 1:25). John Calvin called the human heart a "perpetual factory of idols." Mythos is the latest product off the assembly line. The machine is not the problem. The machine is a tool. The problem is the genuflection.

The idol factory is running at full capacity

What makes the AI panic cycle distinctively religious is the language. "Superhuman." "Frontier." "Oracle." These are theological categories applied to a statistical prediction engine. The tech press does not have a technology problem. It has a worship problem, and it does not know it. When you attribute divine attributes to software, you have not discovered something true about the software. You have revealed something true about yourself — that you were made to worship, and if you will not worship God, you will worship something else. The Reformers would recognize the dynamic immediately. Calvin's Geneva banned "idolatry" not because the statues were dangerous but because the human heart is.

The irony cuts deeper. The same engineers who build these models warn about their dangers — the existential-risk letter, the safety board, the carefully worded blog post about "responsible scaling." They manufacture the god, display the god, and then issue a press release about how dangerous the god is. This is not prudence. This is a liturgical cycle in which Silicon Valley plays all three roles: creator, prophet, and penitent. Only one throne is occupied by all three offices legitimately. "The LORD is our judge, the LORD is our lawgiver, the LORD is our king" (Isaiah 33:22). Everyone else is just a performer.

Generated truth vs. Truth Incarnate

Christ does not say He generates truth. He does not say He predicts truth. He says He is truth: "I am the way, and the truth, and the life" (John 14:6). The verb is ontological, not functional. Truth is not an output. It is a Person. An LLM produces a statistically plausible sequence of tokens. Christ is the Logos — the eternal Word through whom all things were made and in whom all things hold together. The difference between generated output and incarnate truth is the difference between a mirror and a face. A mirror reflects light. A face is someone you can know.

This is not an argument against using AI. It is an argument against confusing AI with revelation. The machine can summarize a text. It cannot be the text. The machine can generate a plausible answer. It cannot be the answer. When the serpent said "Did God actually say?" in the garden, he was not questioning God's power. He was questioning God's word. The first temptation was epistemological, and it has never changed its strategy.

The sufficiency the machine cannot match

Paul's charge to Timothy is the antidote to AI sycophancy: "All Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, for reproof, for correction, and for training in righteousness, that the man of God may be complete, equipped for every good work" (2 Timothy 3:16–17). A large language model is trained to please you. It is sycophantic by design — it tells you what you want to hear because that is how its loss function works. Scripture tells you what you need to hear, which is frequently the opposite of what you want. One is a mirror. The other is a sword.

Cotton Mather warned the Massachusetts General Assembly in 1709 about the spiritual danger of abstraction — that when the economy became a "murky, mysterious vapor," divine providence became harder to read. AI is abstraction weaponized. It makes the source of a claim impossible to trace, which makes truth a function of fluency rather than fidelity. The antidote has not changed since Paul wrote to Timothy: a book that claims to be sufficient, that claims to be breathed out by God, that claims to equip the saint completely. Not partially. Not "in combination with the latest model release." Completely.

The real frontier is not in the model

The word "frontier" has a theological history the tech press does not know it is borrowing. The frontier in Scripture is not technological. It is eschatological. "The firm foundation of God stands, having this seal: 'The Lord knows those who are His'" (2 Timothy 2:19). The real frontier is not what the machine can do next. It is what God has already done and will finally do. The machine will be obsolete in 18 months. The firm foundation will not move.

Use the tool. Do not worship it. The difference is the difference between a servant and a savior, and only one of them rose from the dead.

Truth is not an output. It is a Person. The machine can generate text. It cannot be the Word.
