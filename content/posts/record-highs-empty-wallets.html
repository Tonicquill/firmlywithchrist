The S&P 500 closed at a record high on May 5. The Nasdaq added roughly 1 percent. The Dow rose 0.73 percent. Eighty-three percent of S&P 500 companies that have reported earnings beat estimates. First-quarter earnings growth is now projected to exceed 18 percent, up from 12.8 percent just a month ago. Goldman Sachs raised its year-end target. JPMorgan called it a "broadening rally." The financial press used the word resilient again.

Meanwhile the Federal Reserve's own survey, published in late April, found that 65 percent of Americans say they are worse off financially than they were five years ago. Not slightly worse. Worse. The same survey found that 72 percent of households earning under $75,000 report "financial stress" — up from 58 percent in 2021. The stock market and the person are diverging at a rate that no earnings report can explain.

The divergence is the story. The S&P 500 is weighted toward the ten largest companies, which now account for roughly 36 percent of the entire index. Nvidia, Microsoft, Apple, Amazon, Meta — these are not "the economy." They are a thin slice of it. The median stock in the Russell 2000 index of smaller companies is down roughly 8 percent from its 2021 high. The median worker's real wage is flat. The median household has less in savings than it did before the pandemic. The market is not lying. It is just measuring something else.

Hannah prayed it first: 1 Samuel 2:7-8. The Lord makes poor and makes rich. He brings low and lifts up. Not occasionally. Not as a side effect. As governance. The Westminster Confession of Faith, Chapter 5.1, teaches that God sovereignly orders "all creatures, actions, and things, from the greatest even to the least." The S&P 500 is not outside this. Neither is the gig worker's paycheck. Neither is the parent skipping the doctor's appointment because the deductible is eight thousand dollars. John Calvin wrote that God is "intimately present to all its processes and developments" — meaning wealth disparity is never outside His administrative order. The gap is not an accident. It is not a failure of the system. It is the system, and the system is under God's hand.

The Puritans had a word for this. Cotton Mather, the Boston minister who lived through the birth of paper money, public credit, and the first modern financial experiments in colonial America, warned repeatedly against what he called the "corruptions of the market-place." In a 1709 sermon to the Massachusetts General Assembly, he attacked fraudulent dealings, false weights, usury, and those who "run into debt when they know they can't run out of it." But his deeper concern was spiritual: that the new abstraction of finance — coins that might be counterfeit, paper that might be worthless, credit that might evaporate — was making the workings of providence harder to read. For Mather, money was a metaphor for the soul. It never fully revealed whether it was sound or hollow.

Jesus said it directly: Luke 12:15. Take heed and beware of covetousness, for one's life does not consist in the abundance of the things he possesses. He did not say life is improved by possessions. He said life does not consist in them. The Greek word for consist is zoe — the same word John uses for eternal life. Your life is not measured by your holdings. Your holdings are not even a footnote in the ledger of your soul. The market measures market value. It cannot measure eternal value. It was not designed to.

The doctrine of divine providence says that God governs all things, "from the greatest even to the least." But providence is not a stock-picking strategy. It does not mean the market will go up. It means the market is not the point. The Preacher wrote: Ecclesiastes 4:8. The man has no end to his labors. His eye is not satisfied. He never asks, "For whom do I toil?" The text does not say he is poor. It says he is empty. The market can rise forever and the soul can still be hollow.

For what will it profit a man if he gains the whole world, and loses his own soul?

Mark 8:36

The Bible does not dismiss the market. It dismisses the market as a god. Paul told Timothy: 1 Timothy 6:17. The word uncertain is the same Greek word used for "unreliable" — adēlos, the kind of thing that looks solid until it is not. A record high is not a lie. It is just not a promise. Proverbs 27:24. A crown does not endure. A ticker does not endure. Only the command endures.

James writes: James 5:4. The wages you kept back by fraud cry out. The cries of the reapers have reached the ears of the Lord of Sabaoth. The word cry out is the same word used for Abel's blood in Genesis 4:10 — it means the injustice is not ambient, it is vocal, and God is not deaf. The gig worker's unpredictable paycheck, the parent's skipped appointment, the graduate's debt — these are not statistics. They are cries. And they have reached the ears of the Lord of Sabaoth. The Lord who makes poor and makes rich is also the Lord who hears. This is not a theological abstraction. This is a judicial fact.

Jesus was unambiguous: Matthew 6:24. You cannot serve God and mammon. John Calvin famously taught that the human heart is a "perpetual factory of idols," and market mania is often the most sophisticated factory of all. When the ticker becomes your morning prayer and the portfolio your evening meditation, you have not diversified your assets. You have diversified your worship. Jonathan Edwards, writing in the wake of the 1720 South Sea Bubble collapse that bankrupted Isaac Newton and destroyed half the English gentry, warned that a "counterfeit" love for God was merely "the working of a natural self-love" that seeks Him for "personal gain or material prosperity." Edwards watched the crash from Massachusetts. He was seventeen. He remembered what numbers could do to a soul.

John Calvin, writing in Geneva during the first modern banking experiments, described the heart occupied with "the vain love of the world" as like a ball upon which water is poured: "you can gather, no, not a drop, because there is no empty place to retain the water." The love of God cannot enter a heart that is already full of ticker symbols. Hebrews 13:5. Be content. Not because the market will recover. Because the Master will not leave. The Westminster Shorter Catechism, Q.11, defines providence as God's "most holy, wise, and powerful preserving and governing all His creatures, and all their actions." Even the volatile ones. Even the ones that look like chaos. Even the ones that leave 65 percent of Americans feeling poorer while the index breaks records.

The question is not whether the market is good or bad. The question is whether you can look at a record high and feel peace. If you can, you may be trusting in uncertain riches. If you cannot, you may be trusting in uncertain riches from the other direction — fear instead of greed, but still trust in the wrong object. Providence does not promise that the market will rise. It promises that the market is not in charge. The Lord makes poor and makes rich. He brings low and lifts up. And He has set the world upon its pillars.
