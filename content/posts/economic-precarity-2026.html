The Federal Reserve held interest rates steady in late April at 3.50 to 3.75 percent. The vote was 8 to 4 — the most divided FOMC meeting since October 1992. Chair Jerome Powell, who steps down in mid-May, leaves behind a board that cannot agree on whether the economy needs help or restraint. The Personal Consumption Expenditures price index rose 0.7 percent in March, the biggest monthly gain since June 2022. Year-over-year inflation sits at 3.5 percent. Energy prices, driven by the war in Iran, climbed 24.1 percent in March alone. And yet initial jobless claims fell to 189,000 — the lowest level since 1969.

The numbers say resilient. The feeling says precarious. The gig worker who has not had a predictable paycheck in two years does not read the Fed's statement. The parent who skipped a doctor's appointment because the deductible is eight thousand dollars does not celebrate the jobless claims figure. The graduate with eighty thousand dollars in student loans, working two jobs, does not feel the economy humming. Resilience is a data point. Precarity is a condition.

The gap between the numbers and the feeling is the story. The economy is not in recession. But the anxiety is recession-level. The Fed votes in a room in Washington. People live in kitchens, cars, and emergency rooms. When John Williams, president of the New York Fed, called the economy "remarkably resilient" on May 4, he was speaking from a podium. The person listening was speaking from a parking lot, deciding whether to fill the tank or buy groceries.

The Puritans had a name for this gap. Richard Baxter, a seventeenth-century English pastor, wrote a four-hundred-page manual on melancholy — what we now call depression — and another on economic stewardship. He lived through the English Civil War, the plague, and the Great Fire of London. He watched his country collapse and rebuild. He knew that "resilient" is a word economists use when they mean "not dead yet." Baxter's point was not "do not worry." It was that worry is natural but not sovereign.

The doctrine of divine providence — taught in the Westminster Confession of Faith, Chapter 5 — says that God "sovereignly orders everything... for His own glory." This does not mean the numbers will improve. It means the numbers are not in charge. Baxter called this "the back of the Persian rug" — threads going in chaotic directions until you see the front.

My heart is struck down like grass and has withered; I forget to eat my bread.

Psalm 102:4

The Bible names the feeling before the economist does. The psalmist is not describing a recession. He is describing a soul. The Bible does not dismiss the feeling. It locates it. And it locates it inside a larger frame: not "your anxiety is irrational" but "your anxiety is not in charge."

Genesis records a famine so severe that Genesis 47:15 — the first recorded currency collapse. The Egyptians did not need an economist to tell them the economy was resilient. They needed bread. Joseph did not promise them the money would return. He administered the famine under God's direction, and the people survived what should have destroyed them. James, writing to believers under economic pressure, warned: James 5:4. The word cry out is the same word used for Abel's blood — it means the injustice is not ambient, it is vocal, and God is not deaf. The gig worker's unpredictable paycheck, the parent's skipped appointment, the graduate's debt — these are not statistics. They are cries. And they have reached the ears of the Lord of Sabaoth. Job, after losing everything, said: Job 42:5-6. He did not say "all things are good." He said "now I see." The grammar matters.
