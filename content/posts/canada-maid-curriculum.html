Canada's Medical Assistance in Dying program has been expanding since 2016 like a stain spreading across fabric. Terminal illness first. Then "intolerable suffering" without reasonable prospect of improvement. Then mental illness. Now the province of Ontario has proposed curriculum modules on MAID for high school students — teaching 16-year-olds the eligibility criteria, the application process, the steps from intake to lethal injection. The curriculum materials are written in the flat, informational tone of a driver's education manual. Here are the steps. Here are the forms. Here is how you ask your doctor to kill you.

Disability rights groups, palliative care physicians, and a coalition of parents forced the Ontario government to "pause" the curriculum rollout pending review. The pause is tactical. The ideological machinery behind it is still running. The premise remains unchallenged: the state can teach teenagers that death is a legitimate solution to suffering, and it can do it in the same tone it uses to teach them how to parallel park.

Paul's question to the Corinthians lands on this whole apparatus with the force of a wrecking ball. It does not argue. It disputes the premise. "Do you not know that your body is a temple of the Holy Spirit within you, whom you have from God? You are not your own, for you were bought with a price" (1 Corinthians 6:19–20). The entire MAID framework rests on a single proposition: my body, my choice, my death. Paul's rebuttal does not engage the proposition. It denies its foundation. Your body is not yours. You do not hold title. You were purchased. The cross is a property transaction, and the property is you.

The "neutral" tone that isn't

To teach a 16-year-old the procedural steps for accessing state-facilitated death as though those steps were morally equivalent to obtaining a learner's permit is to teach that death is a consumer choice. The curriculum's neutrality is a performance. It smuggles an entire ethical framework inside bureaucratic language and then claims it is just providing information. This is moral formation in the disguise of civic instruction, and the disguise is the point. If they had to say what they were actually doing — teaching children that suicide is a rational response to hardship — the public relations cost would be too high. So they speak in bullet points and forms instead.

John Calvin would have recognized the maneuver instantly. The Institutes, Book I, Chapter 11, diagnosed the heart as a "perpetual factory of idols," and one of its most efficient productions is the idol that calls itself neutrality. The state that says "we are only providing options" has taken a moral position while denying it holds one. The church cannot afford to be confused by this. The civil magistrate has real but bounded authority. It may govern the body. It may not govern the soul. A curriculum that normalizes death as a rational response to suffering reaches past the body to the soul, and the state has no jurisdiction there. The pause is welcome. The premise behind the pause is still lethal, and Ontario's government has given no indication it considers the premise wrong — merely poorly timed.

You are not your own. You were bought with a price. The body does not belong to the self, and a state that teaches teenagers otherwise — in the tone of a driver's manual — is a state that has forgotten the limits of its jurisdiction.
