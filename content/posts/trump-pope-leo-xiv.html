Donald Trump met Pope Leo XIV at the Vatican on Tuesday. The handshake was photographed. The gifts were exchanged. The readout used the word "cordial" three times, which is diplomatic code for "these two men despise each other but have to stand in the same room." The American president has spent the last year deporting immigrants and cutting foreign aid. The bishop of Rome has spent his pontificate calling for welcome of the stranger and defense of the poor. The photo op required both men to pretend these positions are compatible. They are not.

The awkwardness was visible even through the choreography. Trump governs as if the Sermon on the Mount were a policy suggestion. Leo XIV governs as if it were a binding text. The collision was not personal. It was theological, and neither man can resolve it because neither man can say what Peter said to the Sanhedrin without undermining his own position: "We must obey God rather than men" (Acts 5:29). A president cannot say it because he is the "men" being disobeyed. A pope cannot say it plainly because the Vatican has diplomatic relations to maintain. And both men, whatever their pretensions, are standing under a prior claim: "He is the head of the body, the church" (Colossians 1:18). Not the president. Not the pope. Christ. The jurisdictional declaration that founded the church is the one thing neither of Tuesday's photographed principals can afford to speak aloud.

The borrowed air both men breathe

Christ drew the boundary line without ambiguity: "Render to Caesar the things that are Caesar's, and to God the things that are God's" (Matthew 22:21). Caesar has a sphere. God has a sphere. The boundary is theological before it is political. The medieval theologians spoke of two swords — temporal and spiritual. It was a useful distinction. But the Reformers sharpened the point: the civil magistrate bears the sword of justice, and the church bears the sword of the Spirit which is the Word of God. Neither institution may seize the other's weapon. When a president treats the church as a voting bloc, he dulls his own sword. When a pope treats a photo op as a substitute for prophecy, he sheathes the one he was given. The two men in Tuesday's photograph represent two incompatible understandings of what authority is for, and the one thing they share — the air in their lungs — belongs to someone else.

Two men in a photograph. Both breathe borrowed air. The one who loaned it to them is not in the frame — but He's in the room.
