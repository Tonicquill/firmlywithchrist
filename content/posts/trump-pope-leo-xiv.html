Donald Trump met Pope Leo XIV at the Vatican on Tuesday. The handshake was photographed. The gifts were exchanged. The readout used the word "cordial" three times. The American president, who has spent the last year deporting immigrants and cutting foreign aid, stood beside the bishop of Rome, who has spent his pontificate calling for welcome of the stranger and defense of the poor. The awkwardness was visible even through the diplomatic choreography. Trump governs as if the Sermon on the Mount were a policy suggestion. Leo XIV governs as if it were a binding text. The collision was not personal. It was theological, and the two men represent two incompatible understandings of what the church is for in the political order.

The church has been standing before emperors since before there were emperors. The script was established in Jerusalem, before the Sanhedrin, when Peter declared: "We must obey God rather than men" (Acts 5:29). This is not a refusal to engage with political authority. It is a jurisdictional declaration that precedes and limits all political engagement. The church is not above the state, but it is not under the state either. It is under Christ, and Christ is not on the ballot.

The two swords in the Vatican

Medieval theologians spoke of two swords — the temporal and the spiritual. The Reformers sharpened the distinction. The civil magistrate bears the sword of justice (Romans 13:4). The church bears the sword of the Spirit, which is the Word of God (Ephesians 6:17). Neither institution may seize the other's weapon. When a president treats the church as a voting bloc, and when a pope treats diplomacy as a substitute for prophecy, both swords are dulled.

The Westminster Confession of Faith, Chapter 23, teaches that the civil magistrate "hath authority" in matters of civil order, and that church officers are forbidden from "intermeddling" in civil affairs in ways that usurp the magistrate's office. But the Confession also teaches, in Chapter 31, that the church has the right to "ministerially determine controversies of faith" and to issue counsel and admonition to the civil magistrate. The magistrate rules the body. The church addresses the soul. The line between them is jurisdictional, not architectural — it is drawn by Christ's own distinction between the things that are Caesar's and the things that are God's (Matthew 22:21).

The king who outranks both

Hannah's prayer in 1 Samuel 2 names the real sovereign: "The LORD makes poor and makes rich; He brings low and lifts up" (1 Samuel 2:7). Not the president. Not the pope. The LORD. Trump governs by executive order. Leo XIV governs by encyclical. Both men's authority expires. One will leave office. The other will die. The church's confession is that Christ is already seated at the right hand of the Father, and all earthly authority is borrowed, provisional, and accountable to Him.

"The adversaries of the LORD shall be broken to pieces; against them He will thunder in heaven" (1 Samuel 2:10). This applies to every throne, every palace, every West Wing, every apostolic palace. The judgment is universal because the jurisdiction is universal. No diplomatic readout can soften that reality.

The church's witness is not a press release

The church does not have a foreign policy. It has a confession. "My kingdom is not of this world" (John 18:36) is not a strategy of disengagement. It is the declaration that the church's ultimate allegiance is not available for negotiation, alliance, or photo opportunity. The handshake in the Vatican is not the story. The story is that both men will stand before the same Judge, and only one of them claims to represent Him on earth.

The church does not have a foreign policy. It has a confession. Both the president and the pope breathe borrowed air, maintained by the King whose jurisdiction neither man can escape.
