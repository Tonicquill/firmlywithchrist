Donald Trump met Pope Leo XIV at the Vatican on Tuesday. The handshake was photographed. The gifts were exchanged. The readout used the word "cordial" three times, which is diplomatic code for "these two men despise each other but have to stand in the same room." The American president has spent the last year deporting immigrants and cutting foreign aid. The bishop of Rome has spent his pontificate calling for welcome of the stranger and defense of the poor. The photo op required both men to pretend these positions are compatible. They are not.

The awkwardness was visible even through the choreography. Trump governs as if the Sermon on the Mount were a policy suggestion. Leo XIV governs as if it were a binding text. The collision was not personal. It was theological, and neither man can resolve it because neither man can say what Peter said to the Sanhedrin without undermining his own position: "We must obey God rather than men" (Acts 5:29). A president cannot say it because he is the "men" being disobeyed. A pope cannot say it plainly because the Vatican has diplomatic relations to maintain. The jurisdictional declaration that founded the church is the one thing neither of Tuesday's photographed principals can afford to speak aloud.

The borrowed air both men breathe

Hannah's prayer in 1 Samuel 2 names the real sovereign: "The LORD makes poor and makes rich; He brings low and lifts up" (1 Samuel 2:7). Not the president. Not the pope. The LORD. Trump governs by executive order. Leo governs by encyclical. Both men's authority expires. One will leave office. The other will die. The church confesses that Christ is already seated — the throne is occupied, the administration is staffed, the kingdom is operational — and all earthly authority is borrowed, provisional, and accountable to a King neither of Tuesday's photographed men can overrule.

The medieval theologians spoke of two swords — temporal and spiritual. It was a useful distinction. But the Reformers sharpened the point: the civil magistrate bears the sword of justice, and the church bears the sword of the Spirit which is the Word of God. Neither institution may seize the other's weapon. When a president treats the church as a voting bloc, he dulls his own sword. When a pope treats a photo op as a substitute for prophecy, he sheathes the one he was given. The two men in Tuesday's photograph represent two incompatible understandings of what authority is for, and the one thing they share — the air in their lungs — belongs to someone else.

The church does not have a foreign policy

Christ was unequivocal: "My kingdom is not of this world" (John 18:36). This is not disengagement strategy. It is the declaration that the church's ultimate allegiance is not available for negotiation, alliance, or photo opportunity. The church does not have a foreign policy. It has a confession. It does not issue readouts. It announces the reign of a King whose jurisdiction has no sunset clause. The handshake in the Vatican is not the story. The story is that both men will stand before the same Judge, and only one of them claims to represent Him on earth. The claim will be tested.

Two men in a photograph. Both breathe borrowed air. The one who loaned it to them is not in the frame — but He's in the room.
