A Christian fact-finding team went to Kandhamal district last week. You don't need me to tell you what they found, because it's the same thing every team finds every time anyone bothers to go. Believers beaten with iron rods — check. Women sexually assaulted — check. Pastors' homes torched — check. Police officers who won't file First Information Reports, who treat the victim like the suspect, who see a burned-down church and write "property dispute" on the form — check, check, check. It's been 18 years since the 2008 pogrom killed roughly a hundred Christians and drove fifty thousand into the jungle. Eighteen years. The pattern hasn't budged. The only thing that changes is whether anyone outside India is paying attention that week.

Most weeks, nobody is.

The euphemisms are a second injury. The international press says "communal tension." The Indian government says "isolated incidents." Local officials say "law and order situation." None of them say the word that fits: persecution. The church in Kandhamal is not caught in the crossfire of a conflict between communities. It is the target of a campaign, and the campaign has been running for nearly two decades with the quiet cooperation of the state apparatus that is supposed to stop it.

The road is tribulation — ask Paul

After Paul got stoned and left for dead in Lystra, he got up, went to the next town, and when he eventually circled back through the same cities he told the believers something the Western church has filed under "not applicable": "Through many tribulations we must enter the kingdom of God" (Acts 14:22). Not "around." Not "despite." Through. The suffering is the road, not the ditch alongside the road. Paul's final letter sharpens it to a point: "All who desire to live a godly life in Christ Jesus will be persecuted" (2 Timothy 3:12). All. If your Christianity has never cost you anything, the question isn't "why are they suffering?" It's "what am I doing wrong?"

The FIR that never gets filed

Here's how it works on the ground. A mob shows up at a pastor's house with rods and fuel. The family flees. The house burns. The pastor goes to the police station. The officer behind the desk — who almost certainly shares the mob's religion and possibly its political affiliation — says he'll "look into it." Days pass. The pastor returns. Now the officer says the fire was probably an electrical fault. If the pastor persists, he gets charged under Section 295A of the Indian Penal Code for "outraging religious feelings" — the religious feelings in question being those of the people who burned his house down. This is not incompetence. It is a system functioning as designed.

Acts 5:29 is not an abstract principle for the church in Odisha. It is a survival manual. The state has authority — Paul says so in Romans 13. But the state's authority does not extend to the suppression of the gospel, and when a police officer uses his badge to protect criminals who attack Christ's body, he has stepped outside the jurisdiction God delegated to him. The same Christ who said "render to Caesar" also said "all authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me" (Matthew 28:18). That includes the authority over the officer in Kandhamal who won't take the complaint.

The weapons haven't changed

Revelation says the church conquers "by the blood of the Lamb and by the word of their testimony, for they loved not their lives even unto death" (Revelation 12:11). Not by the blood of the militia. Not by the word of the diplomat. Not by the FIR that finally got filed after enough international pressure made it embarrassing not to. The blood of the Lamb. The word of their testimony. The church in Odisha has been fighting with those two weapons for 18 years. It hasn't lost yet. The persecutor will answer for what he did — "the adversaries of the LORD shall be broken to pieces" (1 Samuel 2:10) — and there is no statute of limitations on that promise.

Eighteen years. Same rods. Same fires. Same officer at the desk who won't take the complaint. And the church is still there — not because it's stubborn, but because Christ promised the gates of hell wouldn't win. They haven't.
