Malaysian Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim called on local councils in early 2026 to address "unauthorised" places of worship. The Malaysian Consultative Council of Buddhism, Christianity, Hinduism, Sikhism, and Taoism — MCCBCHST — is seeking clarification. Many non-Muslim places of worship predate modern land codes. Others operate in shop lots, warehouses, and pastors' homes out of necessity. Orang Asli village churches lack formal registration entirely.

The fear is not abstract. It is the fear that your building will be declared illegal not because it is wrong, but because it is inconvenient. That bureaucratic compliance is being used to suppress minority faith. That the church will be told to move, again, and there will be nowhere to go.

The original wineskin

Luke's description of the primitive church in Acts 2:46–47 places worship in two locations: the temple courts (public, visible, large) and private homes (intimate, domestic, small). The house church is not a downgrade from the cathedral. It is the original wineskin. The believers broke bread "in their homes" — in the dining rooms of ordinary Christians, around ordinary tables, with ordinary food made sacred by the presence of the risen Christ.

Three times Paul greets "the church in your house" (Romans 16:5; 1 Corinthians 16:19; Philemon 1:2). The grammar is precise: not the church that owns a house, but the church that is a house. A living room, a table, a shared meal, the Word read aloud. This is not emergency protocol. It is the biblical pattern.

Where two or three are gathered — there the King is

Christ's promise in Matthew 18:20 — "where two or three are gathered in My name, I am there among them" — is not contingent on zoning approval. The presence of the King does not require a steeple, a permit, or a compliant land code. The shop lot in Kuala Lumpur is as much a church as Notre-Dame, because Christ is as present in the shop lot as He is in the cathedral. The legitimacy of a congregation is Christological, not municipal.

When Mary Tudor burned Protestants and banned their assemblies, the English church did not disappear. It moved into fields, caves, ship holds, and private homes. When the French Huguenots were stripped of their churches, they met in barns and forests. The question is not whether the government recognizes the gathering. The question is whether Christ does — and He has already answered.

A defiant confession

The Reformers who built the Protestant church under threat of burning did not ask for permission. They asked for faithful men. And they built. The Malaysian church's presence in shop lots and village clearings is a confession: "We must obey God rather than men" (Acts 5:29). The confession is made not with placards or petitions but with folding chairs set out every Sunday morning. The church does not seek conflict with the state. It simply continues to be the church, which is its own kind of defiance.

The Westminster Confession of Faith, 25.3, says Christ gave the ministry, oracles, and ordinances "for the gathering and perfecting of the saints." It does not say He gave them for the impressing of the magistrate. The Malaysian government calls these places "unauthorised." The Bible calls them "the church."

A spiritual house of living stones

Peter tells the scattered, displaced, politically marginal church what it actually is: "living stones" being "built up a spiritual house" (1 Peter 2:5). Every Malaysian believer gathering in a rented shop lot, a village clearing, or a pastor's living room is a stone in that temple — quarried by election, placed by the Spirit, immovable by any government decree.

Land codes will change. Governments will change. The temple Christ is building has survived every empire that has ever tried to demolish it. The shop lot in Kuala Lumpur is not a problem to be solved. It is a witness to be celebrated.

The shop lot in Kuala Lumpur is as much a church as Notre-Dame, because Christ is as present in the shop lot as He is in the cathedral.
