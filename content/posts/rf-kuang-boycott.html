R.F. Kuang is the bestselling author of Babel and Yellowface — novels that made her a literary sensation by dismantling colonialism, racism, and the publishing industry's self-flattering myths. Her forthcoming novel, The Assassin's Apprentice, features a protagonist who served in the Israel Defense Forces. The boycott campaign launched before anyone had read the manuscript. The review-bombing on Goodreads began before advance copies shipped. The charge: that any portrayal of an Israeli character that is not explicitly condemnatory "normalizes occupation and genocide." The evidence for the charge: the character's nationality. Nobody making the charge has read the book.

The same literary world that organized readings for Salman Rushdie after the fatwa, that built careers on defending fiction's right to be judged as fiction, that treats authorial freedom as a non-negotiable principle — that world is silent. The silence is the loudest thing about this story. The principle was never "authors should not be harassed for their fiction." The principle was "authors on our side should not be harassed for their fiction." When the protagonist is Israeli, the rules change overnight. Selective moral outrage is not morality. It is tribal signaling in the costume of justice, and the costume is wearing thin.

The mob that judges books it hasn't read

The Ninth Commandment does not contain an exception clause for "unless the cause is righteous." "You shall not bear false witness against your neighbor" (Exodus 20:16). To review-bomb a book you have not opened, to condemn an author for a character's nationality, to assign genocidal intent to fictional events you have not encountered — this is bearing false witness. The commandment does not ask about the banner you carry while doing it. False witness is false witness, whether it comes from the right or the left. The biblical expansion in Exodus 23 is surgical: "You shall not fall in with the many to do evil, nor shall you bear witness in a lawsuit, siding with the many, so as to pervert justice" (Exodus 23:1–2). The mob is the "many." The review-bombing is "siding with the many to pervert justice." The coordinated boycott is the false report. The machinery is ancient. Only the platform is new.

The selective standard is not a bug in the boycott campaign's moral reasoning. It is the entire architecture. The campaigns that would be launched if Kuang had written a protagonist who was a Hamas member, a Ba'athist, or a member of any armed faction aligned with the boycotters' worldview — those campaigns do not exist because the standard does not run both directions. The boycotters have appointed themselves judge, jury, and executioner over what fiction may exist, and they enforce the role with an asymmetry that proves the standard is not justice. It is power. "God shows no partiality" (Romans 2:11). The divine courtroom does not have one docket for allies and another for enemies. The human one does, and the double standard is the confession.

The literary world that finds its courage when the censors are on the right and loses it when the censors are on the left has revealed something it did not intend to reveal. It never believed in free speech. It believed in tribal solidarity, and it called that belief by a nobler name. The church cannot afford to be confused by this. You can disagree with a book's politics without trying to destroy its author. You can critique a fictional character. You cannot condemn a book you have not read without making yourself judge, lawgiver, and king — three offices that were occupied before you arrived.

They review-bombed a novel nobody had read because a fictional character is Israeli. The literary world that defended Rushdie is mute. The principle was never "judge fiction as fiction." It was "protect our own."
