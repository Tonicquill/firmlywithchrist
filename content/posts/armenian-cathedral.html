The Holy Mother of God Cathedral in Stepanakert, Nagorno-Karabakh, was the mother church of the region. It was consecrated in 1889. It survived the Soviet era, the war of the 1990s, and the 2020 conflict. It did not survive April 2026. Azerbaijani forces demolished it. The St. Jacob Church was destroyed alongside it. The European Parliament adopted a resolution April 30 demanding accountability. Azerbaijan's parliament voted May 1 to sever ties with the European Parliament entirely. The first EU-Armenia Summit met in Yerevan May 4-5. Diplomats talked. The cathedral fell.

This is not an isolated incident. The European Centre for Law and Justice notes that over 2,200 anti-Christian incidents were recorded in Europe in 2024 — vandalism, arson, murder of clergy. The Council of Europe passed a resolution on religious discrimination that named antisemitism and Islamophobia explicitly. It did not name anti-Christian prejudice. The cathedral is being erased twice: first by bulldozers, then by institutional silence.

The psalmist wrote: Psalm 74:7-8. They set your sanctuary on fire. They profaned the dwelling place of your Name. They burned all the meeting places of God in the land. This is not metaphor. This is the vocabulary the Bible gives for exactly this event. The psalmist does not say "buildings don't matter." He says their destruction is an attack on God's Name. The Lamentations records: Lamentations 1:10. The nations entered her sanctuary. The foreign army in the holy place. The prophet does not spiritualize it. He names it.

The prophet Isaiah wrote: Isaiah 66:13. The Westminster Confession of Faith, 25.5, calls the church a mother: "there shall be always a church on earth, to worship God according to His will." The mother church in Stepanakert is not the church. But she is not nothing. She is a witness. She is a mother who bore generations of worshippers, marriages, funerals, confessions, communions. When a mother is struck, the children do not say "mothers are just buildings." They weep. And the Bible gives them permission to weep.

They burned all the meeting places of God in the land. We do not see our signs; there is no longer any prophet, and there is none among us who knows how long.

Psalm 74:8-9

Acts 8:1-2. Stephen was stoned. Saul approved. The church was scattered. And devout men made great lamentation. The early church did not say "the Jerusalem church was just a building." They buried Stephen. They lamented. They scattered. And they preached. The scattering was not defeat. It was dispersion. The gospel moved because the church was forced to move. The Holy Mother of God Cathedral in Stepanakert may be rubble. The church in Nagorno-Karabakh is not.

The question is not what UNESCO will do. The question is not what the European Parliament will do. The question is what the church does when its mother is struck. The answer is: it remembers what it is. It is not a building. It is not a nation. It is not a real estate holding. It is the people of God, scattered and gathering, buried and rising, lamenting and preaching. The cathedral fell. The church did not. And the church will outlive every bulldozer, every parliament, every empire that thought erasing stone would erase the Name.
