Ontario wanted to teach 16-year-olds how to die. The backlash paused it.

Ontario proposed MAID curriculum modules for high school students — state-facilitated death taught like driver's education. You are not your own. You were bought with a price.

Sanctity of Life 1 Corinthians 6:19-20 2 Corinthians 4:8-9

Canada's experiment with Medical Assistance in Dying has been expanding since 2016. First it was terminal illness. Then it was "intolerable suffering" without reasonable prospect of improvement. Then mental illness was added to the eligibility list. Now the province of Ontario has proposed curriculum modules on MAID for high school students — teaching 16-year-olds about the availability, eligibility criteria, and process for state-facilitated death. The curriculum materials are written in the neutral, informational tone of a driver's education manual. Here are the steps. Here are the forms. Here is how you ask your doctor to kill you.

The backlash was immediate. Disability rights groups, palliative care physicians, and a coalition of parents forced the Ontario government to "pause" the curriculum rollout pending review. But the pause is tactical, not ideological. The premise remains: the state can teach teenagers that death is a legitimate solution to suffering, and the state can do it in the same tone it uses to teach them how to parallel park.

You are not your own

Paul's question to the Corinthians is the theological foundation that MAID cannot acknowledge and cannot escape: "Do you not know that your body is a temple of the Holy Spirit within you, whom you have from God? You are not your own, for you were bought with a price" (1 Corinthians 6:19–20). The argument for MAID rests on autonomy: my body, my choice, my death. Paul's rebuttal is devastating because it does not argue with the principle. It disputes the premise. Your body is not yours. You do not hold title. You were bought. The cross is a property transaction, and the property in question is you.

This is not an argument for keeping people alive at all costs through aggressive medical intervention against their wishes. Paul is not writing a medical ethics textbook. He is writing theology. The question of the body's ownership is prior to the question of the body's treatment. If the body belongs to the self, MAID is a logical extension of autonomy. If the body belongs to Christ, MAID is a violation of property that is not yours to dispose of.

The state cannot teach what it cannot understand

The MAID curriculum's "neutral" tone is not neutral. It is an ethical claim disguised as an informational one. To teach a 16-year-old the steps for accessing state-facilitated death as if those steps were morally equivalent to the steps for obtaining a learner's permit is to teach that death is a consumer choice among other consumer choices. This is not education. It is moral formation disguised as civic instruction.

The Reformed tradition has always insisted that the civil magistrate has real but limited authority. The state may govern the body but not the soul. A curriculum that normalizes suicide as a rational response to suffering is an act of soul-governance by a state that has no jurisdiction over souls. The pause is welcome. The premise behind the pause is still lethal.

Suffering is not meaningless

Paul's claim about the body is also a claim about suffering. If your body belongs to Christ, your suffering belongs to Christ, and Christ's suffering was not meaningless. "We are afflicted in every way, but not crushed; perplexed, but not driven to despair; persecuted, but not forsaken; struck down, but not destroyed" (2 Corinthians 4:8–9). The logic of MAID says that a life of intolerable suffering is a life not worth continuing. The logic of the cross says that suffering can be redemptive — not because suffering is good, but because the one who suffered for us has entered into suffering and transformed its meaning.

This is not an argument to withhold compassion from the suffering. It is an argument that true compassion does not end the sufferer. It stays with the sufferer. The church's response to MAID must be a community that bears suffering together so convincingly that the state's offer of death is exposed as the impoverished thing it is.

You are not your own. You were bought with a price. The body does not belong to the self, and the state does not get to teach teenagers otherwise.
