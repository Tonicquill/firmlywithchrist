India proposed a Christian Welfare Board. The velvet is a chain.

The bill promises to coordinate, streamline, and facilitate Christian services. But the state that burns churches and then offers to register them is not serving the church. It is managing it.

Ecclesiology Exodus 1:9-10 Matthew 22:21 Daniel 6:10 Colossians 2:8

The government of India has introduced a bill to create a National Christian Welfare Board. The language is bureaucratic benevolence poured thick: the Board will "coordinate," "facilitate," "streamline," and "ensure compliance." It will oversee Christian schools, social services, and charitable activities. The bill's supporters call it overdue recognition of the Christian community's contributions to the nation. The Christian leaders who oppose it — which is most of them — call it what it actually is: a mechanism for state control of the church wearing the language of welfare.

The state that cannot crush the church by force will attempt to domesticate it by regulation. The Board won't shut down churches. It will "register" them. It won't ban Christian education. It will "standardize" it. It won't prohibit charity. It will "coordinate" it. The chains are velvet-lined, and the key is held by the same government whose police officers in Odisha refuse to file First Information Reports when Christians are beaten with iron rods and whose prosecutors in Rajasthan charge Catholic worshippers for the crime of being attacked during Mass. Yesterday's report on Odisha documented this pattern. The day before's report on Rajasthan documented the same pattern. The Welfare Board is the velvet glove on the same iron fist.

The writer of Exodus understood this dynamic cold. When the new Pharaoh surveyed the multiplying Israelites, his diagnosis was not rage. It was demographic calculation: "Behold, the people of Israel are too many and too mighty for us. Come, let us deal shrewdly with them" (Exodus 1:9–10). Pharaoh did not hate the Hebrews. He feared them, and fear wears the mask of prudence better than hatred ever could. The Welfare Board is Pharaoh's playbook adapted for the democratic era: do not kill the Christians. Manage them. Give them a government office with letterhead and a registration number. Make obedience to the state indistinguishable from the administration of charity. The velvet is not an alternative to the chains. The velvet is how the chains are delivered now.

The threshold Caesar cannot cross

Christ's distinction in Matthew 22 is not a negotiation tactic. It is a boundary line drawn by the only person with the authority to draw it: "Render to Caesar the things that are Caesar's, and to God the things that are God's" (Matthew 22:21). Caesar has a sphere. God has a sphere. The boundary is theological before it is political. The state's reach stops at the sanctuary threshold. When the state determines who may preach, what may be taught in Christian schools, and how mercy must be distributed, it has stopped being Caesar. It has become a rival claimant to the headship of the church.

The Welfare Board's registration requirement crosses that line. The Board will not say "we control the church." It will say "we coordinate religious services for the public good." The distinction is semantic and the effect is identical. Daniel served faithfully in the Babylonian civil service for decades — the highest-ranking Hebrew in a pagan empire — and never once protested the legitimacy of the government that employed him. But when the state commanded him to stop praying, he opened his window toward Jerusalem and prayed three times a day, exactly as he had before (Daniel 6:10). He did not issue a statement. He did not organize a petition. He prayed with the window open, and he let the state do what the state was going to do. The Welfare Board would call that noncompliance. Daniel would call it Tuesday.

The Indian church can accept the Board's velvet chains in exchange for legal recognition and state protection. Or it can keep doing what it has done for two thousand years — worship Christ, serve the poor, teach the Word — and accept that the state that offers to register churches today is the same state that allowed them to be burned in 2008. The velvet works only if the church agrees to wear it.

The state that burned churches in 2008 now wants to register them. The velvet is the new delivery mechanism. The chains are the same ones Pharaoh used — but the church has been outlasting Pharaohs since before Pharaoh had a name.
