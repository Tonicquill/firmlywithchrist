Wyoming banned abortion. Not because life is sacred — because the state needs workers.

Wyoming's new abortion law frames children as a state resource to combat demographic decline. The imago Dei is not a labor force projection. A child's value doesn't come from GDP.

Sanctity of Life Psalm 127:3-5 Genesis 1:27 Jeremiah 1:5

Wyoming's governor signed a near-total abortion ban this week. The law will save lives, and that is a genuine moral good. What makes the law worth examining is not the outcome but the justification. The preamble does not mention the image of God. It does not cite the sanctity of life. It cites Wyoming's declining birth rate. An aging population. A shrinking tax base. A workforce shortage. Children, the preamble implies, are a strategic resource whose protection serves the state's economic interest. The law may be righteous. The reasoning behind it is demographic, and demographics change.

The psalmist locates the value of children somewhere the Wyoming legislature cannot see from its committee rooms: "Behold, children are a heritage from the LORD, the fruit of the womb a reward. Like arrows in the hand of a warrior are the children of one's youth" (Psalm 127:3–5). Children are an inheritance from God. Arrows in the hands of parents. Not inputs in a labor-supply model. Not future taxpayers. The psalmist speaks in the language of gift and lineage. The Wyoming legislature speaks in the language of actuarial tables. Those are not the same language, and they fund radically different kinds of protection. A child is not valuable because Wyoming needs workers in 2046. A child is valuable because she is made in the image of her Creator, and that is true whether she will grow up to pay taxes, start a business, or contribute to GDP — or none of those things.

The state that protects for the wrong reasons

The state that grounds human dignity in economic utility is one recession away from reversing itself. Pharaoh wanted Hebrew babies dead for demographic reasons — the Israelites were multiplying, they might join Egypt's enemies, they were a national security risk. Wyoming wants babies alive for demographic reasons — the population is shrinking, the tax base is eroding, the state needs workers. In both cases, the state's interest in the womb is instrumental. The baby is a means. Pharaoh and Wyoming are looking at the same equation from opposite sides, but the equation is the same: human value = economic function. The church can welcome the law's outcome without adopting its logic. More than that. The church must reject the logic precisely because it supports the outcome — if we let the state's utilitarian reasoning pass when it produces results we like, we have forfeited the right to object when the calculus shifts.

God's word to Jeremiah is the definitive rebuttal to demographic reasoning: "Before I formed you in the womb, I knew you, and before you were born I consecrated you" (Jeremiah 1:5). God's knowledge is personal, individual, prior to biology. The state's knowledge is aggregate, statistical, posterior to birth. God knows the unborn child by name. The state counts the unborn child as a future worker. The difference is not one of emphasis. It is one of kind. The imago Dei is an ontological claim about what a human being is, and it cannot be replaced by a labor force projection without losing the thing that makes the claim worth defending in the first place. The child the state protects today for her future economic output is the same child the state may decide tomorrow it cannot afford. The child God protects is protected regardless of output. The church knows which protection is real.

The state that protects children because it needs workers is one downturn away from deciding it no longer needs them. The imago Dei does not fluctuate with GDP.
