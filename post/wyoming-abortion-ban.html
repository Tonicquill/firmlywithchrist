Wyoming banned abortion. Not because life is sacred — because the state needs workers.

Wyoming's new abortion law frames children as a state resource to combat demographic decline. The imago Dei is not a labor force projection. A child's value doesn't come from GDP.

Sanctity of Life Psalm 127:3-5 Genesis 1:27 Jeremiah 1:5

Wyoming's governor signed a near-total abortion ban into law this week. The law's preamble is remarkable — not for what it says about unborn life, but for what it does not say. The text frames the ban in explicitly pro-natalist, demographic terms. Wyoming's birth rate is declining. The population is aging. The state needs workers, taxpayers, residents. Children, the preamble implies, are a strategic resource whose protection serves the state's economic interest. The law may save lives. That is a real and significant moral good. But the justification reveals a state that has mistaken utility for righteousness, and the church should be able to distinguish the two.

A child is not valuable because Wyoming needs workers. A child is valuable because she bears the image of God. The state cannot see the image, so it counts the worker. But the church must insist on the distinction, even when — especially when — the state's error produces outcomes the church agrees with.

Children as heritage, not assets

The psalmist names what the Wyoming legislature cannot: "Behold, children are a heritage from the LORD, the fruit of the womb a reward. Like arrows in the hand of a warrior are the children of one's youth. Blessed is the man who fills his quiver with them" (Psalm 127:3–5). Children are a heritage — an inheritance from God — not a state asset. They are arrows in the hands of parents, not inputs in the economy of a state that needs a larger tax base. The psalmist's language is personal, familial, generational. The state's language is aggregate, demographic, instrumental. The two languages are not compatible, and the church must speak the biblical one even when the state is doing something the church supports for the wrong reasons.

The Reformed tradition's doctrine of common grace applies here with particular force. God can use a secular state's self-interested motives to accomplish morally good ends. The Wyoming law may protect unborn lives that would otherwise have been ended. That is genuine good. But common grace is not justification. The state that protects children because it needs workers is one economic downturn away from deciding it no longer needs them. A state that grounds human dignity in gross domestic product will discover, sooner or later, that some human beings are net drains on GDP.

The image that precedes the economy

The imago Dei is the theological foundation of human dignity, and it is older than every economy: "So God created man in His own image, in the image of God He created him; male and female He created them" (Genesis 1:27). This text is conspicuously absent from the Wyoming law's preamble. The image of God does not appear in demographic projections. It cannot be quantified in labor force participation rates. It does not show up on an actuarial table. The imago Dei is an ontological claim about what a human being is — a creature made in the likeness of the Creator — and it is true whether that human being will grow up to pay taxes, start a business, or contribute to GDP, or none of those things.

The state that replaces imago Dei with economic utility has not found a more pragmatic foundation for human rights. It has replaced a foundation with a calculation, and calculations can change. The market is not a moral argument. It is a price signal, and price signals have no conscience.

I knew you before the state counted you

God's word to Jeremiah is the definitive rebuttal to demographic reasoning: "Before I formed you in the womb, I knew you, and before you were born I consecrated you" (Jeremiah 1:5). God's knowledge is personal, individual, prior to biology. The state's knowledge is aggregate, statistical, posterior to birth. God knows the unborn child by name. The state counts the unborn child as a future worker. Those are not the same kind of knowledge, and they fund radically different kinds of moral reasoning.

Pharaoh wanted Hebrew babies dead for demographic reasons. Wyoming wants babies alive for demographic reasons. In both cases, the state's interest in the womb is instrumental, not moral. The church can welcome the law's outcome while rejecting its logic. And the church must be ready to defend the same unborn life when the state's demographic calculus shifts and the child is no longer an asset but a liability. The child's value does not change when the state's projections do, because the child's value never came from the state's projections.

A child is not valuable because Wyoming needs workers. A child is valuable because she bears the image of God. The state that grounds dignity in GDP is one recession away from reversing itself.
