They stormed the Mass with knives. The police arrested the Catholics.

A mob armed with knives interrupted a Sunday Mass in Rajasthan. The police filed charges — against the Catholics. Acts 4:19-20 was written for this moment.

Persecution Acts 4:19-20 Acts 5:29 1 Samuel 2:6-10 Matthew 16:18

On a Sunday morning in Rajasthan, a group of men armed with knives stormed a Catholic Mass. The congregation fled. The intruders vandalized the sanctuary. When the police arrived, they arrested the Catholics. Not the men with knives. Not the men who interrupted a religious service with weapons in hand. The Catholics — for "unlawful assembly" and "disturbing the peace." The crime, in the eyes of the state, was not the knife-wielding intruder. The crime was the Mass.

This is not an isolated incident. It is the pattern. Across India, Christians who are attacked in worship are then charged with the crimes committed against them. The mechanism is precise: the mob attacks, the police delay, the victims are booked under Section 295A of the Indian Penal Code — "deliberate and malicious acts intended to outrage religious feelings." The Hindu nationalist mob's feelings are, by definition, outraged by the existence of Christian worship. The law is structured so that the victim and the perpetrator are interchangeable. The church in India has learned this lesson the hard way.

Whether it is right to listen to you rather than God

When Peter and John stood before the Sanhedrin — the highest religious-legal authority in Israel — they were ordered to stop speaking in Jesus' name. Their answer was not a negotiation: "Whether it is right in the sight of God to listen to you rather than to God, you must judge, for we cannot but speak of what we have seen and heard" (Acts 4:19–20). The apostles did not deny the Sanhedrin's legal authority. They relativized it. There is a higher court, a higher law, a higher Judge. The Sanhedrin's ruling was legally valid and theologically void, and the apostles were prepared to suffer the consequences of that distinction.

The Indian state's legal machinery is real. The FIR is real. The charges under 295A are real. The jail time is real. And all of it is secondary to the reality that the church will gather, will worship, will preach Christ, regardless of what Section 295A says. "We must obey God rather than men" (Acts 5:29) is not a slogan for the comfortable. It is a legal strategy for the persecuted.

The LORD governs the courtroom

Hannah's prayer in 1 Samuel 2 is the church's political theology in microcosm. "The LORD kills and makes alive; He brings down to the grave and brings up. The LORD makes poor and makes rich; He brings low and lifts up" (1 Samuel 2:6–7). The police officer who files the false FIR does not stand outside this governance. The judge who convicts the victim does not stand outside it. The mob leader who wields the knife does not stand outside it. Every one of them breathes borrowed air, and every one of them will give an account to the Judge whose rulings cannot be appealed.

"The adversaries of the LORD shall be broken to pieces; against them He will thunder in heaven" (1 Samuel 2:10). The police officer in Rajasthan who arrested Catholics while the knife-wielding mob watched, the prosecutor who signed the charging document, the politician who campaigned on "protecting the faith" while enabling attacks on the church — all of them will hear the thunder. And none of them will be able to file a false report in response.

The church in the hands of a sovereign God

The church is not waiting for the Indian judiciary to reform itself. It is not waiting for the international community to notice. It is not waiting for the next election cycle. The church is waiting for the return of the King. While it waits, it worships — in church buildings when it can, in homes when it must, in jail when the state demands it. The location does not change the worship. The charges do not change the confession. The knife does not change the outcome. "On this rock I will build My church, and the gates of hell shall not prevail against it" (Matthew 16:18). The gates of hell include the police stations and the courtrooms and the mobs with knives, and none of them will prevail.

The police arrested the Catholics — not the men with knives. The state's legal machinery is real. But the Judge of all the earth will not be fooled by an FIR.
