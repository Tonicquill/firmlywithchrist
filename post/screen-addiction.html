Your child spends 5 hours a day in worship.

Not at church. On a screen. And the algorithm is a better preacher than you.

Idolatry Exodus 20:3–5 Deuteronomy 6:4–6

The World Happiness Report 2026 just dropped a number that should stop every parent: the average US teen spends nearly 5 hours per day on social media. One in four thirteen-year-olds spends 7 hours or more. That is not entertainment. That is a liturgy.

A liturgy, in the original Greek, means a public practice that forms a person's deepest loves. The Reformers knew this. John Calvin called the human heart a "perpetual factory of idols" (Institutes of the Christian Religion, 1.11.8). The factory has gone digital. Jeremiah 17:9 says: "The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately wicked; who can know it?" The Bible does not treat the heart as a romantic compass. It treats the heart as a production facility — constantly manufacturing substitutes for God. The algorithm merely runs the machinery.

The algorithm does not want your child's money. It wants your child's attention. Psychologists call this an intermittent reinforcement schedule — the same mechanism that powers slot machines. The Bible calls attention a form of worship. Matthew 22:37–38: "You shall love the LORD your God with all your heart, with all your soul, and with all your mind. This is the first and great commandment." For the reader who does not know the Bible: Jesus is asked for the single most important commandment, and He chooses the one that governs what you give your mind to. The greatest commandment is not about emotion. It is about focus. Where your mind goes repeatedly, your worship follows.

Romans 1:23 describes what happens when that focus is redirected: human beings "exchanged the glory of the immortal God for images resembling mortal man." This is not a metaphor about wooden statues. "Images" means anything your hands or your heart manufacture to replace God. The second commandment forbids making a god you can control — including a digital one. When you mold God into your own specifications, Exodus 32 shows the result: about three thousand fell in a single day. The modern equivalent is not bodily death. It is spiritual anesthesia — the slow erasure of the capacity for genuine prayer, genuine friendship, and genuine joy.

You shall not make for yourself a carved image, or any likeness of anything that is in the heaven above, or that is in the earth beneath, or that is in the water under the earth. You shall not bow down to them or serve them. For I the LORD your God am a jealous God.

Exodus 20:3–5

The Reformed tradition has a doctrine for this: the Sabbath. Not as a rule, but as a gift. WCF 21.7: "The Sabbath is kept holy unto the Lord when men... do not only observe an outward rest, but... are taken up the whole time in the public and private exercises of His worship." The Sabbath is the one day when the algorithm does not own your child. The question is whether the parent has the courage to enforce it.

Colossians 3:5 adds a further claim: "Put to death your members which are on the earth: fornication, uncleanness, passion, evil desire, and covetousness, which is idolatry." For the reader who does not know the Bible: Paul is writing to ordinary people in a Roman city, and he calls covetousness — the relentless desire for more — idolatry. The algorithm's entire business model is covetousness. It is designed to make you want the next thing before you have finished the current thing. That is not neutral technology. That is idolatrous architecture.

1 John 2:15–17 says: "Do not love the world or the things in the world... For all that is in the world — the lust of the flesh, the lust of the eyes, and the pride of life — is not of the Father but is of the world." The algorithm targets all three. The lust of the flesh through appetite content. The lust of the eyes through infinite scroll. The pride of life through metrics, likes, and comparison. It is a systematic assault on the very categories Scripture warns against.

Haidt and Rausch's research shows heavy social media use (5+ hours/day) is linked to roughly double the risk of depression compared to light use (<1 hour). Reducing use for just one week causally lowers depression, anxiety, and loneliness in experiments. The data is not ambiguous. The behavior is.

You do not need a new app to fix this. You need an old commandment.
