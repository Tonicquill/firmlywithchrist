May 1, 2026

Your child spends 5 hours a day in worship.

Not at church. On a screen. And the algorithm is a better preacher than you.

Idolatry Exodus 20:3–5 Deuteronomy 6:4–6

The World Happiness Report 2026 just dropped a number that should stop every parent: the average US teen spends nearly 5 hours per day on social media. One in four thirteen-year-olds spends 7 hours or more. That is not entertainment. That is a liturgy.

A liturgy, in the original Greek, means a public practice that forms a person's deepest loves. The Reformers knew this. In the Reformed tradition, idolatry is a technical term: it means anything that captures the heart's ultimate allegiance. John Calvin called the human heart a "perpetual factory of idols" (Institutes of the Christian Religion, 1.11.8). The factory has gone digital.

The algorithm does not want your child's money. It wants your child's attention. Psychologists call this an intermittent reinforcement schedule — the same mechanism that powers slot machines. The Bible calls attention a form of worship. Deuteronomy 6:4–6: "Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God, the LORD is one. You shall love the LORD your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your might. And these words that I command you today shall be on your heart." The text assumes that what you set before your eyes repeatedly will shape what you love. That is why Israel was commanded to bind God's words "as a sign on your hand" and "as frontlets between your eyes." What you look at, you become. What you scroll through, owns your attention. And what owns your attention, directs your worship.

You shall have no other gods before me. You shall not make for yourself a carved image, or any likeness of anything that is in the heaven above, or that is in the earth beneath, or that is in the water under the earth. You shall not bow down to them or serve them. For I the LORD your God am a jealous God.

Exodus 20:3–5

For the reader who does not know the Bible: this is not a metaphor about wooden statues. "Carved image" means anything your hands or your heart manufacture to replace God. The second commandment forbids making a god you can control — including a digital one. The Hebrew word behind the translation describes a covenant partner's rightful claim to exclusive fidelity, like a marriage vow. God is saying: I made a covenant with you. I do not share my covenant partners with substitutes. The algorithm is a rival suitor. That is why the commandment exists.

The Reformed tradition has a doctrine for this: the Sabbath. Not as a rule, but as a gift. WCF 21.7: "The Sabbath is kept holy unto the Lord when men... do not only observe an outward rest, but... are taken up the whole time in the public and private exercises of His worship." The Sabbath is the one day when the algorithm does not own your child. The question is whether the parent has the courage to enforce it.

Haidt and Rausch's research shows heavy social media use (5+ hours/day) is linked to roughly double the risk of depression compared to light use (<1 hour). Reducing use for just one week causally lowers depression, anxiety, and loneliness in experiments. The data is not ambiguous. The behavior is.

You do not need a new app to fix this. You need an old commandment.
