1 in 3 women. The church told her to submit more.

The CDC published the numbers this year. The Reformers had a different answer.

Justice Genesis 1:27 Ephesians 5:25

The CDC published its 2023–2024 National Intimate Partner and Sexual Violence Survey this year. The numbers are not new. They are persistent. More than 1 in 3 women in the United States — nearly 43.5 million — have experienced contact sexual violence, physical violence, or stalking by an intimate partner. Nearly 1 in 3 of those women reported lasting impact: injury, PTSD, missed work, needed medical care. The Global Burden of Disease Study 2023 puts the worldwide figure at 608 million women aged 15 and older. Domestic violence is the fourth-leading risk factor for health loss among women globally. It is not a private matter. It is a public health crisis. And the church has been part of the problem.

Here is what too many churches have said to the woman with a black eye: pray harder. Submit more. Do not deprive your husband. God hates divorce. The texts are real. The application is demonic. Ephesians 5:22–23 has been weaponized by abusers and by pastors who have never read the verse that follows it. The same paragraph — the same breath — says: "Husbands, love your wives, as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her." For the reader who does not know the Bible: the standard for husbands is not leadership as dominance. It is leadership as crucifixion. Christ did not dominate the church. He died for it. Any man who uses Ephesians 5 to demand submission while ignoring the command to die is not preaching the Bible. He is preaching himself.

For the LORD God of Israel says that He hates divorce, for it covers one's garment with violence.

Malachi 2:16

Read that again. God hates divorce because it covers the garment with violence. The violence is the treachery. The violence is what shreds the covenant. When a pastor tells a beaten woman that "God hates divorce" to keep her in the house, he has it backwards. God hates the violence that made the divorce necessary. The text says take heed to your spirit, that you do not deal treacherously. The treacherous dealer is the abuser, not the woman who flees.

Husbands, love your wives, as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her.

Ephesians 5:25

Husbands, love your wives and do not be bitter toward them.

Colossians 3:19

Two separate letters. One command repeated: love. And a warning added: do not be bitter. The word is not "do not disagree." It is not "do not lead." It is do not be bitter — a specific prohibition against the resentment and harshness that fuel abuse. The Bible does not leave this vague. It names the sin.

The Reformed tradition has a doctrine that speaks directly to this: the imago Dei — the image of God. Genesis 1:27 says male and female are both created in God's image. For the reader who does not know the Bible: this is not a spiritual platitude. It is a legal claim about human worth. To strike a woman is to strike God's likeness. To degrade her is to degrade the One whose image she bears. The sixth commandment — "You shall not murder" — is not about avoiding homicide. The Heidelberg Catechism (Q.105) asks what God requires, and the answer is: "That I neither in thoughts, nor words, nor gestures, much less in deeds, revile, hate, insult, or kill my neighbor." The catechism calls this "the second table" — the commands that govern how humans treat humans. Domestic violence is not a marriage problem. It is a sixth-commandment problem.

1 Peter 3:7 adds a further constraint. The text calls the woman the "weaker vessel" — a phrase abusers love. But the sentence does not end there. It says husbands must show her honor "since they are heirs with you of the grace of life." She is not a subordinate. She is an equal heir of God's grace. And the text ends with a warning: if a husband fails to honor her, his prayers are hindered. The man who demands submission while withholding honor is not a spiritual leader. He is a man whose own prayers God refuses to hear. That is not pastoral opinion. That is the apostle Peter.

The Puritans — often caricatured as harsh — were actually clearer on this than many modern churches. William Ames (1576–1633), one of the most influential Puritan ethicists, wrote: "If one party drive away the other with great fierceness and cruelty, there is cause of desertion, and he is to be reputed the deserter." Ames is saying that a man who beats his wife until she flees is not the abandoned spouse. He is the one who deserted the marriage covenant — by violence, by cruelty, by making the home unlivable. The Westminster Confession (24.6) permits divorce for "wilful desertion as can no way be remedied by the Church, or civil magistrate." The Reformers who wrote that document understood that desertion is not only physical absence. It is the creation of conditions that make presence impossible.

For the reader who does not know the Bible: this is a reversal. The text that has been used to trap women in violent marriages — "God hates divorce" — was never meant to protect abusers. The Reformers allowed divorce for adultery and for irremediable desertion. William Ames argued that cruelty that drives a spouse out makes the cruel spouse the formal deserter. The church's job is not to keep marriages intact at the cost of lives. The church's job is to protect the image of God wherever it is attacked — including inside a marriage certificate.

You shall not afflict any widow or fatherless child. If you afflict them in any way, and they cry at all to Me, I will surely hear their cry; and My wrath will become hot, and I will kill you with the sword; your wives shall be widows, and your children fatherless.

Exodus 22:22–24

For the reader who does not know the Bible: this is not metaphor. This is the voice of God in the law of Moses, speaking to a nation where women and children had no legal standing. God says: I will hear their cry. Not "I will hear after you file the right paperwork." Not "I will hear if the elders agree." I will surely hear their cry. And the consequence for the abuser is not counseling. It is not a suspension from the worship team. It is the sword. God takes the side of the afflicted against the one who afflicts them. That is not a human invention. That is divine law.

Learn to do good; seek justice, rebuke the oppressor; defend the fatherless, plead for the widow.

Isaiah 1:17

Give justice to the weak and the fatherless; maintain the right of the afflicted and the destitute. Rescue the weak and the needy; deliver them from the hand of the wicked.

Psalm 82:3–4

So what does the church do? Not vague prayer. Concrete protection. James 1:27 says pure religion is to visit the afflicted in their trouble. That means showing up at the safe house with groceries, not a Bible study. That means the church's money pays for the lock change, not just the mission trip. The command is not "pray for them." The command is visit them in their trouble.

Here is the prescription: believe the victim without requiring a court verdict first; offer immediate physical safety through shelter networks, not as charity but as justice; remove the abuser from leadership and from the Lord's Table, not as punishment but as protection of the flock; counsel the victim toward legal protection without pressuring her to "save the marriage"; and preach Ephesians 5:25 as loudly as Ephesians 5:22 — so that no man in the congregation can claim ignorance. Romans 13:4 calls the civil magistrate God's minister, an avenger to execute wrath on the evildoer. Calling the police is not a failure of faith. It is an act of submission to the ordinance God appointed for the protection of the innocent. The Reformers called the church a "mother" (WCF 25.5). A mother does not hand her daughter back to the man who broke her ribs. A mother shelters, feeds, and defends.

The gospel is not for the proud. It is for the broken. And the woman who limps into church after years of violence is not a failed wife. She is a sinner for whom Christ died, now bearing scars that Christ did not cause. The church must say this plainly: the scars are not her sin. They are someone else's. And the One who judges justly will not ask her why she did not submit more. He will ask her abuser why he struck the image of God.
