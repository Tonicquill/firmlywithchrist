R.F. Kuang wrote a novel with an Israeli character. The boycott came before anyone read it.

The author of Babel faces a review-bombing campaign over a fictional character's nationality. The same literary world that defended Rushdie is silent. Exodus 20:16 applies to review-bombers too.

Cultural Engagement Exodus 20:16 Exodus 23:1-2 Matthew 7:1-2 Romans 2:11 Deuteronomy 1:17 Isaiah 33:22

R.F. Kuang is the bestselling author of Babel and Yellowface — novels that made her a darling of the literary left by skewering colonialism, racism, and the publishing industry's hypocrisies. Her upcoming novel, The Assassin's Apprentice, features a protagonist who served in the Israel Defense Forces. The boycott campaign began before anyone had read the book. The review-bombing started on Goodreads before advance copies shipped. The charge is that any sympathetic portrayal of an Israeli character "normalizes occupation and genocide." The evidence for the charge is the character's nationality.

The same literary circles that defended Salman Rushdie against a fatwa, that organized readings for authors targeted by the right, that built their brand on the principle that fiction deserves to be judged as fiction — those circles are silent. The silence is not an oversight. It is a revelation. The principle was never "authors should not be harassed for their fiction." The principle was "authors on our side should not be harassed for their fiction." When the target is an Israeli character, the rules change. Selective moral outrage is not morality. It is tribal signaling dressed in the language of justice, and the language is lying.

You shall not bear false witness

The Ninth Commandment is short and absolute: "You shall not bear false witness against your neighbor" (Exodus 20:16). To review-bomb a book you have not read, to condemn an author for a character's nationality, to assign genocidal intent to a fictional portrayal you have not encountered — this is bearing false witness. It is not political critique. It is lying about a neighbor's work in order to destroy their livelihood, and the Commandment does not contain an exception for "unless the cause is just." False witness is false witness. The mob's certainty does not make it true.

The biblical expansion of the commandment is explicit: "You shall not join hands with a wicked man to be a malicious witness. You shall not fall in with the many to do evil, nor shall you bear witness in a lawsuit, siding with the many, so as to pervert justice" (Exodus 23:1–2). The mob is the "many." The review-bombing is the "false report." The coordinated boycott campaign is "siding with the many" to "pervert justice." The law of God has already diagnosed the entire dynamic. It does not matter which flag the mob carries. The diagnosis is the same.

With the measure you use

Christ's warning about judgment is not a prohibition on discernment. It is a prohibition on selective standards: "Judge not, that you be not judged. For with the judgment you pronounce you will be judged, and with the measure you use it will be measured to you" (Matthew 7:1–2). The boycott campaign applies a measure to Kuang that it would never apply to an author whose protagonist was a Hamas fighter, a Ba'athist, or a member of any other armed group aligned with the boycotters' politics. The measure is asymmetrical, and the asymmetry proves that the standard is not justice. It is power.

Paul makes the point theologically explicit: "God shows no partiality" (Romans 2:11). The God of the Bible does not have a double standard — one for allies and one for enemies, one for the tribe and one for the outsider. Partial judgment dressed as moral clarity is an old sin, and the Bible has had a name for it since Deuteronomy: "You shall not be partial in judgment. You shall hear the small and the great alike" (Deuteronomy 1:17). The boycott campaign hears the great — the activists, the influencers, the ideological allies — and refuses to hear the author at all.

One occupies all three branches. It is not the mob.

Isaiah 33:22 is the definitive statement of divine prerogative: "For the LORD is our judge, the LORD is our lawgiver, the LORD is our king; He will save us." When human beings claim all three functions — when they decide what is permitted (lawgiver), pronounce guilt (judge), and execute punishment through economic pressure (king) — they are usurping a throne that belongs to God alone. The boycott campaign is not a community exercising discernment. It is a mob exercising sovereignty, and sovereignty over what may be written belongs to no human court.

The literary world that rushes to defend speech when the censors are on the right and falls silent when the censors are on the left has revealed that it never believed in free speech. It believed in tribal solidarity. The church must be able to say: you can disagree with a book's politics without trying to destroy its author. You can critique a character without having read the book, but you cannot condemn a character without having read the book. And you cannot condemn an author for a character's nationality without having made yourself the judge, lawgiver, and king — three offices that are already occupied.

The same literary world that defended Rushdie is silent now. The principle was never "fiction should be judged as fiction." It was "authors on our side should not be harassed." That is not justice. It is tribalism in moral drag.
