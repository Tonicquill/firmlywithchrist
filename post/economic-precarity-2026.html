Resilient on paper. Precarious in person.

Economic anxiety 2026: Fed says resilient, but inflation and energy prices leave Americans feeling precarious. The Puritans had a word for this.

The Federal Reserve held interest rates steady in late April at 3.50 to 3.75 percent. The vote was 8 to 4 — the most divided FOMC meeting since October 1992. Chair Jerome Powell, who steps down in mid-May, leaves behind a board that cannot agree on whether the economy needs help or restraint. The PCE price index rose 0.7 percent in March, the biggest monthly gain since June 2022. Year-over-year inflation sits at 3.5 percent. Energy prices, driven by the war in Iran, climbed 24.1 percent in March alone. And yet initial jobless claims fell to 189,000 — the lowest level since 1969.

The numbers say resilient. The feeling says precarious. The gig worker who has not had a predictable paycheck in two years does not read the Fed's statement. The parent who skipped a doctor's appointment because the deductible is eight thousand dollars does not celebrate the jobless claims figure. Resilience is a data point. Precarity is a condition.

When the money failed

Genesis records the first documented currency collapse in Genesis 47:15: "When the money failed in the land of Egypt." The Egyptians did not need an economist to tell them the economy was resilient. They needed bread. The text is brutally matter-of-fact — money fails. It has always failed. It will fail again. The question is not whether your financial foundation can shake. The question is whether your soul has foundations deeper than the economy.

Joseph did not promise the Egyptians their money would return. He administered the famine under God's direction, and the people survived what should have destroyed them. Economic precarity is not a modern invention. It is a historical constant. The Bible does not pretend otherwise, and neither should the church.

My heart is struck down like grass

The psalmist names the feeling before the economist does: "My heart is struck down like grass and has withered; I forget to eat my bread" (Psalm 102:4). This is not a description of a recession. It is a description of a soul. The Hebrew verb nakah — struck down, smitten — is the same word used for military defeat. Economic precarity is a kind of violence. It strikes the heart the way an army strikes a city. The body keeps going to work while the soul has already surrendered.

Richard Baxter, the 17th-century English pastor who lived through civil war, plague, and the Great Fire of London, wrote a four-hundred-page manual on what we now call depression. He watched his country collapse and rebuild. He knew that economic anxiety is not weakness. It is the natural response of a creature to a world where the foundations keep moving. Baxter's counsel was not "stop worrying." It was: your worry is real, and it is not sovereign. There is a difference between feeling precarity and being ruled by it.

The wages you kept back by fraud cry out

James delivers the verdict in James 5:4: "The wages of the laborers which you kept back by fraud cry out. And the cries of the reapers have reached the ears of the Lord of Sabaoth." The word "cry out" is krazein — the same verb used for Abel's blood crying from the ground. The injustice is vocal, and God is not deaf. When employers classify full-time workers as contractors to avoid benefits, when wages stagnate while productivity rises, when the economy posts "record highs" while 65 percent of Americans feel worse off — these are not market inefficiencies. They are sins, and they are being recorded by a Judge who does not accept earnings reports as evidence for the defense.

Cotton Mather warned the Massachusetts General Assembly in 1709 against "false weights, usury, and those who run into debt when they know they can't run out of it." His deeper concern was spiritual: that the abstraction of finance was making providence harder to read. Mather saw a world where coins might be counterfeit, paper might be worthless, and credit might evaporate — and the soul had to be anchored somewhere deeper than any of it.

The LORD makes poor and makes rich

Hannah's prayer in 1 Samuel 2:7–8 is the anchor: "The LORD makes poor and makes rich; He brings low and lifts up." Not "the market makes poor and makes rich." Not "the Fed makes poor and makes rich." The LORD. The verb is active, present-tense divine governance. The same God who raised Jesus from the dead governs whether your rent check clears. This does not mean the rent will clear. It means the outcome is not random, not cruel, and not beyond the reach of the One who "raises the poor from the dust and lifts the beggar from the ash heap."

Providence does not promise that the numbers will improve. It promises that the numbers are not in charge. The Triune King who governs all things — from the greatest to the least — governs the 3.5 percent inflation, the 0.7 percent monthly PCE jump, the 8-to-4 FOMC vote, and the single mother who skipped her appointment. He governs all of it. None of it escapes His hand. And all of it is moving toward a resolution where every injustice is addressed and every debt is either paid or forgiven.

Providence does not promise the numbers will improve. It promises the numbers are not in charge. The LORD makes poor and makes rich — and He raises the poor from the dust.
