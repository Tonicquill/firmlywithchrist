$42 million for costumes while Christians sat in an Indian jail.

The Met Gala raised $42M for the Costume Institute. The same week, Catholics were arrested in Rajasthan and Christians were beaten in Odisha. The blessed are not on the red carpet.

Providence James 5:1-5 John 18:36 Amos 6:4-6 Matthew 5:10

The Met Gala raised $42 million on Monday night. Celebrities ascended the museum steps in outfits costing more than most Americans earn in a year. The theme was "Radical Forms: Fashion and the Avant-Garde." The press called it "dazzling," "iconic," "a triumph." The photographs traveled around the world in seconds. The red carpet was immaculate.

The same week, in Rajasthan, India, Catholic worshippers were arrested after a mob armed with knives stormed their Sunday Mass. In Odisha, a Christian fact-finding team documented beatings, sexual assault, and police complicity — 18 years after a pogrom killed nearly 100 believers. Neither story made the red carpet. Neither story raised $42 million. The persecuted church does not have a dress code, a step-and-repeat, or Anna Wintour. It has the blood of the Lamb and the word of its testimony, and in the economy of the kingdom, that is worth more than the entire Met Gala endowment.

The apocalyptic doom of corrupted riches

James speaks to the Met Gala with a directness the church has spent centuries trying to soften: "Come now, you rich, weep and howl for the miseries that are coming upon you. Your riches have rotted and your garments are moth-eaten. Your gold and silver have corroded, and their corrosion will be evidence against you and will eat your flesh like fire" (James 5:1–3). James is not being hyperbolic. He is being eschatological. The corrosion of the gold is not a metaphor. It is a witness for the prosecution. Every dollar spent on a dress that will be worn once, photographed, and archived is a dollar that will testify in the divine courtroom about what its owner valued and what its owner ignored.

The indictment sharpens: "You have lived on the earth in luxury and in self-indulgence. You have fattened your hearts in a day of slaughter" (James 5:5). The day of slaughter is not a future event in James' grammar. It is the present arrangement of the world, in which the rich feast while the righteous suffer, and the feasting is itself the evidence that the feasters do not understand what time it is. The Met Gala is not a party. It is a pageant of spiritual blindness.

The kings of the earth and the King of heaven

Jesus stood before Pilate — the representative of the world's most powerful empire, a man with the legal right to kill Jesus or release Him — and said: "My kingdom is not of this world" (John 18:36). The Met Gala is the kingdom of this world displayed in its fullest regalia. The church in Rajasthan, gathered in a modest building while a mob sharpens knives outside, is the kingdom of God displayed in its fullest faithfulness. The two kingdoms are not allies. They are not even in the same genre. One is a fashion show. The other is the body of Christ.

Amos preached to the ruling class of the northern kingdom of Israel — prosperous, secure, culturally sophisticated — and delivered the most savage class analysis in Scripture. "Woe to those who lie on beds of ivory and stretch themselves out on their couches... who drink wine in bowls and anoint themselves with the finest oils — but are not grieved over the ruin of Joseph" (Amos 6:4–6). The sin is not the ivory. The sin is not the wine. The sin is the indifference. The Met Gala is ivory and wine and oil and music, and the ruin of Joseph — the suffering body of Christ, the arrested Catholics, the assaulted women in Odisha — is not on the guest list.

The blessed are not on the red carpet

Jesus delivered the definitive inversion of the world's value system from a mountainside in Galilee: "Blessed are those who are persecuted for righteousness' sake, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven" (Matthew 5:10). Not the rich. Not the famous. Not the well-dressed. The persecuted. The Greek word is dediogmenoi — those who are pursued, harassed, hunted. The ones in Rajasthan who fled from the knife-wielding mob. The ones in Odisha who sleep in the jungle because their homes were burned. These are the blessed, and the kingdom of heaven belongs to them. The Met Gala has $42 million. The persecuted church has the kingdom. The exchange rate is not close.

The Met Gala raised $42 million for costumes while Christians in Rajasthan were arrested for attending Mass. The blessed are not on the red carpet. They are in the dock.
