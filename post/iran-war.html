May 1, 2026

2,362 dead. 217 of them children.

The UN reported the toll from Iran this week. The Bible's response to civilian death is not "God has a plan." It is "How long, O Lord?"

Lament Psalm 44 Psalm 88

The UN Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs reported the numbers on April 16: since Operation Epic Fury began on February 28, at least 2,362 Iranian civilians have been killed. More than 32,000 injured. Among the dead: 217 children. A missile strike on an elementary school killed nearly 170 people. The numbers are two weeks old. They are higher now.

The Christian response to civilian death is not "God has a plan." It is not "pray for peace." It is lament. One-third of the Psalter is complaint. Here is Psalm 44:23–24: "Awake, O Lord! Why do you sleep? Rouse yourself! Do not reject us forever. Why do you hide your face and forget our misery and oppression?" That is not a lack of faith. That is faith screaming at God. God put that question in the Bible on purpose — so His people would know that accusation is a valid form of prayer.

Awake, O Lord! Why do you sleep? Rouse yourself! Do not reject us forever. Why do you hide your face and forget our misery and oppression?

Psalm 44:23–24

The Reformed tradition has a robust theology of lament. It does not require the mourner to clean up their language before approaching God. It does not require theological correctness in grief. It requires only honesty. Psalm 88 is the darkest prayer in the Bible. The psalmist says he has been crying out to God "day and night" and God has done nothing. He says God's wrath "lies heavy" upon him. He says he is "afflicted and close to death." And here is how the psalm ends — the final verse, the last word:

You have caused my beloved and my friend to shun me; my companions have become darkness.

Psalm 88:18

No resolution. No "but God." No turnaround. Just darkness. And the Holy Spirit included it in Scripture anyway. For the reader who does not know the Bible: this means the church's own songbook contains a prayer that dies in the dark. The Reformers took that seriously. Lament is not doubt. It is doctrine.

Providence says God governs even the horrors — not that He authors them, and not that He delights in them. The Westminster Confession says God "ordains whatsoever comes to pass" (WCF 3.1) while also being "neither the author of sin nor the approver of it" (WCF 5.4). That tension is not resolved in a single sentence. That is the point. Lament says we are allowed to say the horror out loud. Providence says the horror is not outside God's governance. Both are true. Neither cancels the other. Romans 8:28 promises that "for those who love God all things work together for good." The promise is for those who are in Christ, not a commentary on geopolitics for spectators.

The dead in Iran do not need our analysis. They need our grief.
