Record highs. Empty wallets.

S&P 500 hits record high while most Americans feel poorer than ever. Hannah prayed about this gap before the Fed existed.

Providence 1 Samuel 2:7-8 Ecclesiastes 4:8 Matthew 6:24 James 5:4 1 Samuel 2:10

The S&P 500 closed at a record high on May 5. The Nasdaq added roughly 1 percent. The Dow rose 0.73 percent. Eighty-three percent of S&P 500 companies that have reported earnings beat estimates. First-quarter earnings growth is projected to exceed 18 percent, up from 12.8 percent a month ago. Goldman Sachs raised its year-end target. JPMorgan called it a "broadening rally." The financial press used the word resilient again.

Meanwhile the Federal Reserve's own survey, published in late April, found that 65 percent of Americans say they are worse off financially than they were five years ago. Not slightly worse. Worse. Seventy-two percent of households earning under $75,000 report "financial stress" — up from 58 percent in 2021. The stock market and the person are diverging at a rate no earnings report can explain.

The LORD makes poor and makes rich

Hannah's prayer in 1 Samuel 2:7–8 is the most politically incorrect text in Scripture: "The LORD makes poor and makes rich; He brings low and lifts up." Not occasionally. Not as a side effect. As governance. The Westminster Confession of Faith, Chapter 5.1, teaches that God sovereignly orders "all creatures, actions, and things, from the greatest even to the least." The S&P 500 is not outside this jurisdiction. Neither is the gig worker's paycheck. Neither is the parent skipping the doctor's appointment because the deductible is eight thousand dollars.

John Calvin wrote that God is "intimately present to all its processes and developments" — meaning the widening gap between index highs and kitchen-table anxiety is not an accident. It is not a failure of the system. It is the system, functioning exactly as it was designed, and the system is under God's hand. The hand that holds the nail-scarred palm also holds the ledger. There is no corner of the economy where the Triune King's decree is not law.

The loneliness of the market ticker

The Preacher's scalpel in Ecclesiastes 4:8 cuts to the bone: "There is one alone, without companion. He has neither son nor brother. Yet there is no end to all his labors, nor is his eye satisfied with riches." The text does not say he is poor. It says he is alone. The market can rise forever and the soul can still be hollow. The S&P 500 closing at a record high is a measurement of aggregate corporate profit. It is not a measurement of anything that matters at the level of the human heart.

The median stock in the Russell 2000 index of smaller companies is down roughly 8 percent from its 2021 high. The median worker's real wage is flat. The median household has less in savings than it did before the pandemic. The market is not lying. It is just measuring something that has almost nothing to do with whether a family can sleep at night.

The sin of serving "resilience"

When New York Fed President John Williams called the economy "remarkably resilient" on May 4, he was speaking from a podium. The person listening was speaking from a parking lot, deciding whether to fill the tank or buy groceries. "Resilience" is a word economists use when they mean "not dead yet." It is not a word that translates into the language of human survival.

Jesus stated the irreducible economic theology of the kingdom in Matthew 6:24: "You cannot serve God and money." The verb is douleuein — to serve as a slave. Christ does not say money is a bad servant. He says it is a rival master. When the ticker becomes your morning prayer and the portfolio your evening meditation, you have not diversified your assets. You have diversified your worship. Cotton Mather, preaching to the Massachusetts General Assembly in 1709, warned against what he called "the corruptions of the market-place" — fraudulent dealings, usury, and the spiritual danger of abstract finance that made providence harder to read. Money, Mather insisted, was a metaphor for the soul: it never fully revealed whether it was sound or hollow.

The judicial weight of economic inequity

James delivers the verdict in James 5:4: "The wages of the laborers which you kept back by fraud cry out. The cries of the reapers have reached the ears of the Lord of Sabaoth." The Greek word translated "cry out" is krazein — the same verb used for Abel's blood crying from the ground in Genesis 4:10. The injustice is vocal. God is not deaf. The gig worker's unpredictable paycheck, the single mother's skipped medical appointment, the graduate's debt that compounds faster than salary — these are not statistics. They are testimonies, and they have reached the ears of the Lord of Hosts.

Hannah's prayer closes with a promise: "The adversaries of the LORD shall be broken in pieces; from heaven He will thunder against them" (1 Samuel 2:10). Every CEO who suppressed wages to inflate a quarterly earnings beat, every board that approved a buyback while laying off workers, every analyst who called it "efficiency" while 65 percent of Americans said they were worse off — none of it escapes the bench of Christ. There is no statute of limitations on economic injustice in the divine courtroom.

Providence does not promise the market will rise. It promises the market is not in charge. The cries of the reapers have reached the ears of the Lord of Sabaoth.
