Four churches. One week. Zero headlines.

Four churches burned in Chhattisgarh while the world scrolled past. Hannah's prayer says the God who permits the fire will also raise the poor from its ashes.

Persecution 1 Samuel 2:6-8 Matthew 5:10-12 1 Peter 2:5 1 Samuel 2:10

Four churches. One week. Zero international headlines. In Chhattisgarh, central India, Hindu extremists burned church buildings to the ground — again — and the world's response was the sound of scrolling past.

The pattern has become so predictable it barely registers as news. Accusations of "forced conversions" — a charge that in India means "you helped a Dalit who chose Christ over caste." Rallies outside police stations. Demands for demolition. Then the flames. The camera crews don't come. The diplomatic statements don't issue. The church in Chhattisgarh worships in the open air on Sunday morning, and the global body of Christ hits "like" on a brunch photo.

The hand that permits the fire

Hannah's prayer in 1 Samuel 2:6–8 is the most politically incorrect text in Scripture: "He raises the poor from the dust and lifts the beggar from the ash heap." The ash heap in Chhattisgarh this week is literal. A church building reduced to cinders. And Hannah insists — not politely, but prophetically — that the same God who will raise these believers from that ash permitted the fire to touch it first.

This is not fatalism. Fatalism says nothing matters. Sovereignty says everything matters — so much so that no spark, no mob, no extremist leader operates outside the Triune King's decree. The fire did not slip through God's fingers. It answered to Him. This is either the most terrifying thing you have ever heard or the most comforting. For the believer in Chhattisgarh who has nothing left but the open sky and the gathered saints, it is the latter.

Blessed are you when men revile you

Jesus did not give the Beatitudes at a conference. He gave them on a hillside to people who would soon watch their Teacher executed. "Blessed are those who are persecuted for righteousness' sake, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven" (Matthew 5:10–12). The word is makarioi — not "happily fortunate" but "divinely, irreversibly favored." The burning church in Chhattisgarh is, by Christ's own calculus, the blessed church.

The West's confusion on this point is total. We treat suffering as a sign of divine abandonment. The New Testament treats it as identification with Christ.

The temple they cannot touch

Peter tells the scattered church that they are "living stones" being "built up a spiritual house" (1 Peter 2:5). The temple in Jerusalem was destroyed in AD 70 and has never been rebuilt. The church has never stopped being built. The difference is the Builder. No empire has ever successfully dismantled what Christ constructs.

The arsonist in Chhattisgarh can reduce a physical structure to rubble in an afternoon. He cannot touch a single living stone. The gathered saints — singing psalms under the open sky because their roof is gone — are the temple he is actually trying and failing to destroy.

No crime unrecorded, no flame unjudged

"The adversaries of the LORD shall be broken in pieces," Hannah concludes. "From heaven He will thunder against them" (1 Samuel 2:10). The arsonist who struck the match in Chhattisgarh this week has not escaped the jurisdiction of the Judge. Every crime that escapes the Indian courts will be prosecuted in full at the bench of Christ.

The church does not need the world's attention. She has something better than a headline. She has a verdict.
