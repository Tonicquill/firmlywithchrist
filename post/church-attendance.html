70 people showed up. It took 25 years.

The Hartford Institute found median church attendance rose for the first time in decades. The Bible's question is different: why do they gather?

Ecclesiology Hebrews 10:24-25 Acts 2:42

The Hartford Institute for Religion Research published its findings this month. For the first time in the survey's history, median in-person worship attendance at U.S. congregations increased — from 65 people in 2020 to 70 in 2025. The researchers called it "cautious optimism." Headlines called it a reversal. Church leadership blogs called it a win.

Here is the harder question: Does God care about the number? The Bible does not measure the church by headcount. It measures the church by what happens when the heads are counted together. The command to gather is not a demographic target. It is a divine design.

And let us consider one another in order to stir up love and good works, not forsaking the assembling of ourselves together, as is the manner of some, but exhorting one another, and so much the more as you see the Day approaching.

Hebrews 10:24-25

For the reader who does not know the Bible: this is not a rule about showing up. It is a command about one another. "Consider one another." "Stir up love." "Exhort one another." The purpose of gathering is not the gathering. The purpose is the building up of God's people by God's people. The text says to do this more as the end approaches — not because attendance scores matter to heaven, but because the pressure on believers increases as the culture darkens, and you cannot endure alone what God designed you to endure together.

Acts 2:42 describes the first church. They did not gather for entertainment, for programs, or for demographic relevance. They gathered for four things: the apostles' doctrine, fellowship, the breaking of bread, and prayers. These are what the Reformed tradition calls the means of grace — the ordinary channels through which God distributes what His people need. Not extraordinary signs. Not personal revelations. Romans 10:17 says faith comes by hearing. Hearing requires a voice. A voice requires a speaker. A speaker requires an assembly. God designed it this way on purpose.

I write so that you may know how you ought to conduct yourself in the house of God, which is the church of the living God, the pillar and ground of the truth.

1 Timothy 3:15

For the reader who does not know the Bible: the "house of God" is not a building. It is the gathered people. And the text calls them the pillar and ground of the truth. A pillar holds something up. A ground anchors it. The church exists to hold up the truth of God's Word in a world that constantly pulls it down. This is not a social function. It is a structural one. When the church stops preaching the truth, the culture loses its anchor. When the church starts preaching something else — self-help, politics, entertainment — it is no longer a pillar. It is a billboard.

The Westminster Confession of Faith (25.3) says Christ gave the visible church "the ministry, oracles, and ordinances of God, for the gathering and perfecting of the saints." The Reformers did not believe the church saves anyone. Only Christ does that. But they believed the church is the place where Christ has appointed His Word to be preached and His sacraments to be administered. To neglect the assembly is not merely to miss a service. It is to miss the ordinary means God uses to feed His people.

God does not need full pews. His people need each other. And they need His Word.

So what does the 5-person increase mean? Not a revival. Not a movement. A signal. Some people still believe that God meets His people when they gather around His Word. The question is not whether attendance rose. The question is whether the churches they returned to are still churches — places where the apostles' doctrine is taught, where fellowship means mutual exhortation, where the breaking of bread means the Lord's Supper, and where prayer is not a performance but a plea.

The warning is for the churches too. Do not celebrate the number while betraying the design. A full building with empty preaching is worse than an empty building with faithful preaching. The Reformers would rather have a small congregation hearing the Word than a large congregation hearing themselves. Hebrews 10:25 is not a growth strategy. It is a command from God to His people: gather, exhort, stir up love, and do it more as the Day approaches. Not because the institution needs you. Because you need the means of grace. And God is the one who appointed them.
