They burned a convent and called a patriarch a traitor.

A convent in Yaroun was demolished. A patriarch was attacked for not picking a side. The church is not a nation. It does not have a foreign policy.

Ecclesiology John 18:36 Revelation 12:11 Hebrews 13:14

On May 4, 2026, Melkite Greek Catholic bishops in Lebanon condemned the destruction of a Melkite convent in the village of Yaroun, southern Lebanon. The convent was reportedly demolished by Israeli forces operating in the area. The bishops called it a "deep wound in the national and human conscience." Two days later, on May 5, Hezbollah supporters launched a campaign of abuse against Syriac Maronite Patriarch Mar Bechara Boutros al-Rai, accusing him of treason and collaboration with Israel. Eastern patriarchs — Assyrian Church of the East, Syriac Catholic, Chaldean — condemned the attacks as an attempt to incite sectarian strife. Lebanese authorities ordered security agencies to identify those responsible.

The church in the Middle East is being squeezed from both sides. Physical destruction on one side. Rhetorical destruction on the other. The expectation is that Christians will pick a team in a war they did not start. The reality is that the church is not a team. It is not a nation. It does not have a foreign policy.

Jesus said it before Pilate: John 18:36. My kingdom is not of this world. If it were, my servants would fight. The church in Lebanon has no army. It has no tanks. It has no lobby in Washington or Tehran. It has the Word, the sacraments, and the promise that the gates of hell will not prevail. The Westminster Confession of Faith, 25.3, says the church's job is the ministry, oracles, and ordinances — not geopolitical alignment. When Patriarch al-Rai is attacked for not being political enough, the irony is that his job is to be theological, not political.

John saw it in his vision: Revelation 12:11. They conquered him by the blood of the Lamb and by the word of their testimony. Not by the blood of the militia. Not by the word of the diplomat. The blood of the Lamb. The word of their testimony. The church in the Middle East has been conquering this way for two thousand years — through empires that rose and fell, through caliphates that rose and fell, through mandates and wars and revolutions. The convent in Yaroun is not Lebanese property or Israeli property. It is church property. And the church belongs to Christ.

Here we have no lasting city, but we seek the city that is to come.

Hebrews 13:14

The author of Hebrews says: Hebrews 13:14. Here we have no lasting city. The church in Lebanon is not building a lasting city in Yaroun. It is building a testimony. The convent was a witness. Its destruction is a witness. The patriarch's refusal to pick a side is a witness. The Eastern patriarchs' solidarity is a witness. The church's survival in the Middle East is not a political achievement. It is a theological one. Augustine wrote in The City of God that the church is a pilgrim people, not a national institution. The pilgrim does not defend territory. The pilgrim defends the testimony.

The question is not whose side the Lebanese church is on. The question is whether the church remembers what it is. It is the people of God, scattered and gathering, buried and rising, lamenting and preaching. It is the pilgrim city, passing through the earthly city, bearing witness to the heavenly one. The convent fell. The testimony did not. The patriarch was attacked. The testimony did not. And the testimony will outlive every army that thought burning stone would silence the Word.

The church in the Middle East has no army. It has the blood of the Lamb and the word of its testimony. And it has outlived every empire that thought it could be silenced.
