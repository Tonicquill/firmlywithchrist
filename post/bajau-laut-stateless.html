The Bajau Laut Must Convert to Belong. The Bible Calls That Coercion.

Off the coast of Sabah, an indigenous maritime people are stateless, undocumented, and invisible to the state. To be seen, they must be classified as Muslim. Their homes were burned in 2024. Their children beg on the streets in 2026. The Bible has something to say about what happens when a nation conditions belonging on creed.

Off the eastern coast of Sabah, Malaysia, in the turquoise waters of the Sulu and Celebes Seas, an indigenous maritime people called the Bajau Laut have lived for centuries on wooden houseboats and stilt-huts above the coral. They are born on the water. They die on the water. They can free-dive to depths of sixty metres and hold their breath for minutes at a time — a physiological adaptation scientists have traced to enlarged spleens developed over generations of marine life. They have no birth certificates, no identity cards, no passports, and no citizenship from any nation on earth. The modern borders drawn between Malaysia, the Philippines, and Indonesia simply passed over them. They are, in the language of international law, stateless. In the language of the Bible, they are exactly the kind of people God commands nations to protect and exactly the kind of people nations most reliably crush.

In June 2024, Malaysian enforcement officials demolished and burned the stilt-homes of over five hundred Bajau Laut families across seven islands in the Tun Sakaran Marine Park near Semporna. Some residents received notice. Some watched their houses set alight without warning. Crops were destroyed. Families scattered — some to Semporna town, some to rocky outcrops on the beach, some to makeshift canvas shelters with no protection from the weather. The government cited security concerns, illegal structures, and environmental violations. It later emerged that a RM478 million township was planned for the area. In October 2024, activist Mukmin Nantang of the advocacy group Borneo Komrad was arrested under Malaysia's Sedition Act for defending the community. By April 2026, Bajau Laut children had become a visible presence on the streets of Kota Kinabalu, Kudat, and Semporna — begging, collecting recyclables, and in some cases sniffing glue to stave off hunger. The Sabah Welfare Department had taken 298 children into temporary protection centres between 2023 and mid-2025.

The evictions made international news. The deeper story — the one that predates the bulldozers and the burnings and will outlast them — is about what a state demands in exchange for the right to exist inside its borders. The Bajau Laut are not merely homeless. They are administratively invisible. And the only path toward visibility runs through a single door.

The theology of the identity card

The Bajau Laut are classified as Muslims by the Sabah Islamic Affairs Department (JHEAINS). The Sabah Mufti, Datuk Bungsu Aziz Jaafar, has stated plainly that the community is formally Muslim, though he acknowledges that their actual religious practice is a syncretic blend of Islam and traditional ancestral spirit-worship — a reality born of centuries of isolation from formal religious instruction of any kind. When popular preacher Ebit Lew visited Pulau Tatagan in Semporna in early 2021, he reported that villagers told him no one had ever explained religion to them. Lew distributed food aid — their supply had been cut off — and then led 157 Bajau Laut in reciting the shahadah, the Islamic declaration of faith. He called it a mass conversion. The Sabah Mufti confirmed its validity. Lew pledged to build a school and send an ustaz to instruct the new converts. The conversions stood. The school never materialised. The aid did not return.

This is the pattern. Material desperation meets religious requirement. The Bajau Laut need food, housing, healthcare, education, and the legal right to exist in the only place they have ever known. All of these require documentation. Documentation requires religious classification. The classification is already decided. To be seen by the Malaysian state as a Bajau Laut is, by default, to be seen as a Muslim. If you are a Christian Bajau Laut — and a Christian Bajau Laut community does exist, documented in a 2025 academic study titled "Dari Laut ke Salib" ("From the Sea to the Cross") published in the Indonesian theological journal EPIGRAPHE — you face a choice the state will not name but enforces with perfect consistency. Accept the religious identity the system assigns you, or remain outside the system entirely.

The mechanism is not a form that says "convert here for citizenship." It is more effective than that. Malaysia's National Registration Department has for decades classified indigenous Sabahans as Muslim on their MyKad identity cards based solely on the presence of "bin" or "binti" (son of / daughter of) in their names — patronymic markers common to Malay Muslim naming conventions but also widely used by Sabah's indigenous Christian peoples, including Dusun, Kadazan, Murut, and Rungus communities. This practice, which activists call "MyKad Islamisation," changes a person's legal religious identity without their knowledge or consent. In January 2026, the Kota Kinabalu High Court finally ruled the practice "fundamentally wrong." Judge Datuk Celestina Stuel Galid found in favour of a man named Abdul Manap bin Bakusai and his three children, allowing correction of their MyKad from Islam to Christianity. But the ruling addressed one family. The administrative machinery that produced the error remains intact. For the Bajau Laut — who often lack any documentation at all — the problem is even more acute. They are not correcting a wrong classification. They are seeking to exist in the system at all, and the system only has one box for them.

The legal architecture of one-way conversion

Malaysia's legal framework creates an asymmetric religious marketplace. Muslim preachers and organisations are free to conduct dakwah (Islamic missionary outreach) toward non-Muslims — including the Bajau Laut, whose Muslim status the state asserts but whose actual religious formation the state acknowledges is minimal. At the same time, it is illegal for non-Muslim religions to proselytise toward Muslims. Since the Bajau Laut are classified as Muslims, Christian churches and organisations cannot legally provide religious outreach to them — even to those who are Christian by conviction rather than by administrative decree. The state's classification becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy. The Bajau Laut are Muslim because the state says so. Because the state says so, only Islamic religious instruction is permitted. Because only Islamic instruction is permitted, the community's religious future is engineered in one direction.

This is not an accident of bureaucracy. It is the logical endpoint of a system in which religious identity is a function of state documentation rather than personal conviction. The Westminster Confession of Faith addresses this with surgical precision: "God alone is Lord of the conscience, and hath left it free from the doctrines and commandments of men which are in anything contrary to His Word, or beside it, in matters of faith or worship" (WCF 20.2). The confession's authors had witnessed states using the machinery of civil power to coerce religious conformity. They wrote chapter 20 to foreclose it. A faith produced by administrative classification is not faith. A shahadah recited because the food supply was cut off is not confession. It is survival behaviour dressed in religious language — and the people who facilitate it know exactly what they are doing. "Woe to you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! For you travel across sea and land to make a single proselyte, and when he becomes a proselyte, you make him twice as much a child of hell as yourselves" (Matthew 23:15). Jesus was not describing evangelism. He was describing the manufacture of religious identity through pressure, incentive, and social engineering. The methods change. The condemnation does not.

The God who names the unnamed

Scripture is not silent about people like the Bajau Laut. It is startlingly specific. "You shall not pervert the justice due to the sojourner or to the fatherless, or take a widow's garment in pledge, but you shall remember that you were a slave in Egypt and the LORD your God redeemed you from there; therefore I command you to do this" (Deuteronomy 24:17–18). "When a stranger sojourns with you in your land, you shall not do him wrong. You shall treat the stranger who sojourns with you as the native among you, and you shall love him as yourself, for you were strangers in the land of Egypt: I am the LORD your God" (Leviticus 19:33–34). The command carries a motive clause: you were strangers. Israel's law embedded a permanent memory of statelessness into its treatment of the stateless. You do not crush the sojourner because you were a sojourner. You do not exploit the undocumented because you were undocumented. The memory of Egypt was meant to immunise Israel against becoming Egypt.

Malaysia has no such memory encoded into its legal treatment of the Bajau Laut. What it has is Project IC — a decades-long scandal in which immigrants were allegedly granted citizenship and voting rights en masse during the 1970s through the 1990s for the purpose of shifting Sabah's demographic and electoral balance. The operation was widely understood to have a sectarian calculus: increasing the Muslim voter base to support a particular political party. The Bajau Laut, who have a legitimate indigenous claim to belong, are now paying the price for a political operation they had nothing to do with. The citizenship debate is frozen by the trauma of a prior injustice, and the Bajau Laut freeze with it — stateless because the state fears repeating a sin it already committed against different people in a different way. This is what happens when a nation treats religious identity as a tool of demographic engineering. The tool eventually becomes the gate, and the gatekeeper eventually forgets that the people outside the gate are people.

"For our citizenship is in heaven, from which we also eagerly wait for the Savior, the Lord Jesus Christ" (Philippians 3:20). Paul wrote this as a Roman citizen addressing a Roman colony — people who understood exactly what citizenship meant as a legal status and a political privilege. He did not tell them earthly citizenship was worthless. He told them it was provisional. The Bajau Laut's lack of a passport does not derail their status as image-bearers or their eligibility for adoption as sons. But the Bible never uses the promise of heavenly citizenship as an excuse for earthly injustice. "If a brother or sister is poorly clothed and lacking in daily food, and one of you says to them, 'Go in peace, be warmed and filled,' without giving them the things needed for the body, what good is that?" (James 2:15–16). Telling the Bajau Laut their citizenship is in heaven while their houses burn and their children sniff glue on the streets of Kota Kinabalu is not eschatological hope. It is evasion with a Bible verse attached.

What the state owes the stateless

The Reformed tradition has never been anarchist. It has always affirmed that civil magistrates are ordained by God for specific purposes: the punishment of evildoers and the protection of those who do good (WCF 23.1; Romans 13:1–4). When a state demolishes the homes of a stateless people, denies them documentation, permits only one religious community to offer them aid, and arrests the activists who advocate for them, it is not governing. It is persecuting. The fact that the persecution wears the clothes of administrative procedure rather than overt violence makes it harder to photograph but no easier to justify. The Bajau Laut cannot vote the government out. They cannot sue for their rights in courts that do not recognise their legal existence. They cannot withhold their labour — they are already excluded from formal employment. The power relationship between the Malaysian state and the Bajau Laut is about as close to absolute as a modern state-citizen relationship can get. Absolute power over a population with no recourse is not a test of governance. It is a test of character, and the character being revealed is not flattering.

The Sabah government has argued that security concerns, environmental protection, and the prevention of illegal immigration justify its actions. Those concerns are not fabricated. The Sulu Sea has genuine security challenges. Marine conservation is a legitimate state interest. Cross-border migration does complicate citizenship determinations. But none of these justify burning people's homes and offering them no alternative. None of them justify conditioning humanitarian relief on the recitation of a creed. None of them justify a thirty-year failure to resolve the legal status of a people who were there before the borders were drawn. "He who oppresses the poor to increase his riches, and he who gives to the rich, will surely come to poverty" (Proverbs 22:16). The proverb does not distinguish between oppression by violence and oppression by bureaucracy. The outcome is the same.

The church and the invisible

The academic study of Christian Bajau Laut describes their situation with painful restraint: they face "eviction, administrative discrimination, and minimal church support." The last phrase should land with particular weight on anyone reading this who belongs to a confessional Christian church. Minimal church support. A people pressured toward religious conformity by the combined weight of state classification, aid conditionality, and legal prohibition on non-Islamic outreach — and the global church's response has been, by the researchers' own assessment, minimal. This is not a Malaysian problem. It is a body-of-Christ problem. When one part of the body suffers, every part suffers with it (1 Corinthians 12:26), and the Bajau Laut have been suffering in near-total ecclesial invisibility.

The scholars who studied the Christian Bajau Laut recommended a pastoral model that is "incarnational, dialogical, and advocacy-based" — one that pursues not only spiritual nurture but access to education, healthcare, and social protection. That is a Reformed recommendation whether the authors used the word or not. The gospel is not merely a message about spiritual salvation for the soul. It is the announcement that Jesus is Lord over bodies, borders, identity cards, and housing policy — and the church that confesses His lordship is obligated to act like it matters for the people whom earthly lords are crushing. "But Peter and the other apostles answered and said: 'We ought to obey God rather than men'" (Acts 5:29). For the Bajau Laut who are Christian, obedience to God means refusing to recite a creed they do not hold in exchange for benefits the state should provide regardless. For the church outside Malaysia, obedience to God means refusing to be silent about a situation that is designed to remain invisible. For the Malaysian church, obedience to God means advocating for a system in which documentation and religious identity are not the same transaction — a system in which the state can see a Bajau Laut without deciding what god she must belong to.

The Bajau Laut do not need a shahadah. They need a birth certificate. The state has made them the same thing. That is not governance. It is coercion wearing an administrative name tag, and the God who hears the cry of the sojourner is not confused about which it is.
