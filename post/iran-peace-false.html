They announced peace in Iran. Jeremiah 6:14 has the rebuttal.

The ceasefire was signed. The diplomats said 'peace.' But the rubble in Tehran is still there. The 217 dead children are still dead. A ceasefire is a legal instrument — not a resurrection.

Providence Jeremiah 6:14 1 Samuel 2:6-7 Matthew 16:26

The ceasefire was announced on May 3. Operation Epic Fury, the US-led bombing campaign that killed at least 2,362 Iranian civilians including 217 children, is formally over. Diplomats called it "the end of hostilities." The UN Security Council praised "restraint." Oil futures dropped. Airline stocks rallied. The machinery of normalcy resumed its normal grinding. The word "peace" was deployed. Nobody asked the parents of the 217 dead children whether they felt peaceful.

A ceasefire is a legal instrument. It is not a resurrection. The rubble in Tehran has not been cleared. The elementary school hit by a missile has not been rebuilt. The parents of dead children are still parents of dead children. Jeremiah delivered the standing indictment against this exact form of bureaucratic optimism: "They have healed the wound of my people lightly, saying, 'Peace, peace,' when there is no peace" (Jeremiah 6:14). The wound is not healed. The bandage is a press release. Jeremiah was addressing prophets who served the palace narrative rather than God's truth — the ancient equivalent of the security-clearance briefers who tell presidents what they want to hear. The dynamic has not changed in 2,600 years.

The bodies are still being counted

When diplomats say "peace," they mean "the bombing has stopped." When the grieving say "peace," they mean "give me my child back." Those are not the same word. They are not even in the same language. The ceasefire is real — the bombs have stopped falling. The ceasefire is a lie — nothing has been put back together. Both things are true. The church's job is not to choose which truth to tell. It is to hold both, to acknowledge the tactical reality and refuse the moral fraud.

States make war for mixed motives — some legitimate, some criminal. States make peace for mixed motives too. The ceasefire is not pure. The war was not pure. Hannah's prayer cuts through both: "The LORD kills and makes alive; He brings down to the grave and brings up. The LORD makes poor and makes rich; He brings low and lifts up" (1 Samuel 2:6–7). While diplomats and generals clock out, He does not. While the UN Security Council issues a statement and moves to the next agenda item, He is still watching the mother in Tehran who cannot sleep because the bedroom is too quiet. That mother is not a policy variable. She is a person under the direct observation of the living God, and her child's death did not escape His notice. Neither will the general who ordered the strike escape His judgment.

The court date is not cancelled

Christ's question to the disciples applies as directly to the UN Security Council as it did to them: "For what will it profit a man if he gains the whole world and forfeits his soul?" (Matthew 16:26). Every participant in this war and its ceasefire — every pilot, every general, every diplomat, every president and supreme leader — gained something from the deal. Strategic position. Oil price stability. A headline with the word "peace." And every one of them will stand before a Judge whose jurisdiction has no sunset clause. The ceasefire memorandum does not cancel the court date. The UN statement does not enter the record as mitigating evidence. What did they gain? What did they forfeit? The silence of the missing children is louder than the entire diplomatic record.

A ceasefire is a legal instrument. It is not a resurrection. The bombs have stopped. The dead are still dead. There is no peace where there is no restoration — and nothing has been restored.
