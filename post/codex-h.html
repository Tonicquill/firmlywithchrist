May 1, 2026

Lost for 500 years.

Monks used 42 pages of a 6th-century Bible as scrap paper. Scientists just found them.

Providence Isaiah 40:8 WCF 1.8

Scientists at the University of Vienna announced the recovery of Codex H — a manuscript of Paul's letters from the 6th century, dismantled at Mount Athos in the 1200s, its pages reused as "guard sheets" to protect other books. The ink was invisible to the naked eye. The parchment had been scraped, written over, folded, and forgotten.

Multispectral imaging revealed the text underneath. The words were still there.

The Westminster Confession of Faith calls this the preservation of Scripture: "The purity of Scripture being in all ages kept pure in the church." (WCF 1.8) The Confession does not say the church preserves the text by its own vigilance. It says the text's purity is kept pure in the church — implying divine agency working through, not merely dependent upon, human custodians. This does not mean every manuscript is identical — textual scholars have documented thousands of variants. It means the text's substance has survived despite human carelessness, political opposition, and monks who used it as scratch paper. Preservation is not the absence of damage. It is the survival of the message through the damage.

The grass withers, the flower fades, but the word of our God will stand forever.

Isaiah 40:8

God does not need human reverence to preserve His Word. He works through careless monks, scrap paper, and 500 years of darkness. The text survived heresy, war, book burnings, and monks who used it as scratch paper. Not because humans were faithful. Because God was.
