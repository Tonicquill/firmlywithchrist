The mob came with iron rods.

Five churches in Kandhamal burned this week. The crime, again, is the name of Christ. Hannah's prayer is a war cry for exactly this.

Persecution 1 Samuel 2:6-7 Acts 14:22 Matthew 16:18 1 Samuel 2:10

The mob came with iron rods. Five churches in Odisha's Kandhamal district, reduced to splinters and ash. The year is 2026. The crime, again, is the name of Christ.

India's Supreme Court sits in Delhi. The constitution guarantees religious freedom. And in a district still scarred by the 2008 massacres — when Hindu nationalists killed nearly 100 Christians and drove tens of thousands into the jungle — the church buildings went up in flames this week with the same impunity they always have.

The state mechanism notices nothing. The international press files no dispatch. The global Christian conscience is, once again, elsewhere.

The LORD kills and makes alive

Hannah's prayer in 1 Samuel 2:6–7 is not a soft word for a soft people. It is a war cry. "The LORD kills and makes alive; He brings down to the grave and brings up." That verb — kills — is not accidental. Yahweh is sovereign over the mob, sovereign over the iron rod, sovereign over the official who looked away. The same hand that holds the nail-scarred palm holds the universe. The mob does not escape His jurisdiction because it carries a different flag.

The Hindu nationalist who swings the rod breathes borrowed air. His pulse is maintained by the God he denies. This is not abstract theology. It is the specific comfort of the persecuted church. The one who strikes you cannot act outside the permission of the one who saves you.

This is what the apostles signed up for

The early church did not gather to build a political base. "We must through many tribulations enter the kingdom of God" (Acts 14:22). The Greek word — thlipsis — means pressure, crushing, tribulation that grinds. Paul lists beatings, stoning, shipwrecks, "in perils among false brethren" as apostolic credentials, not apostolic failures.

When the West treats persecution as a missional problem to be solved rather than a mark of the church to be expected, it fundamentally misreads the Book it claims to believe.

Stone buildings. Living Church.

"On this rock I will build My church," Christ declared, "and the gates of Hades shall not prevail against it" (Matthew 16:18). Not "on these five structures in Kandhamal." Not "on this permit from the district magistrate." Jesus builds with living stones, not brick and mortar. A church building can burn in an hour. The Church cannot burn at all — because it is built by hands that hold the flame.

No iron rod in Odisha touched a single living stone this week. The buildings are replaceable. The gathered saints are not.

The Judge is also the Avenger

Hannah's prayer does not end with mere sovereignty. It lands on specific judgment: "The adversaries of the LORD shall be broken in pieces; from heaven He will thunder against them" (1 Samuel 2:10). The God who numbers the hairs of His people also numbers the crimes of their persecutors. There is no statute of limitations on the divine bench.

The church in Odisha is not abandoned. She is not forgotten. And her enemies are not getting away with anything.
