340,000 people are praying over glass.

Notre-Dame wants to replace 19th-century stained glass with contemporary art. 340,000 people signed a petition. The Reformers would understand why.

Ecclesiology Psalm 27:4 Psalm 96:9 Colossians 2:8 Matthew 16:18

President Emmanuel Macron and Archbishop Laurent Ulrich want to replace six 19th-century stained-glass windows by Eugène Viollet-le-Duc in Notre-Dame de Paris with contemporary creations by artist Claire Tabouret. Heritage group Sites et Monuments has filed an urgent legal appeal. Over 340,000 people have signed a petition to preserve the historic glass. Father Michel Viot called for peaceful on-site protests, calling the plan an "attack on beauty." France's National Commission for Heritage and Architecture issued a negative opinion. The dispute is not about restoration. It is about who decides what sacred space looks like.

The argument is theological whether the participants know it or not. Viollet-le-Duc's windows are not mere decoration. They are catechesis in glass — biblical scenes arranged to teach, to narrate, to form the viewer in the grammar of salvation. Replacing them with contemporary art is not renovation. It is a statement about whether tradition has authority.

To gaze upon the beauty of the LORD

David's singular ambition in Psalm 27:4 was to "behold the beauty of the LORD." The Hebrew word for beauty is no'am — from a root meaning pleasantness, delight, agreeableness. Beauty is not optional in biblical religion. It is a form of knowledge. The Reformers, who destroyed idolatrous images but filled their churches with the Psalms sung in four-part harmony, understood this: beauty persuades before the sermon begins.

Viollet-le-Duc's windows are not merely old. They are theological arguments rendered in color and light. Each biblical scene is a sermon in silica — a narrative grammar that has formed worshippers for more than a century. The contemporary replacement says: the present moment is the final authority over what the church looks like. The historic windows say: the gospel is a story that unfolds across generations, and no single generation gets to rewrite it.

Worship the LORD in the beauty of holiness

Psalm 96:9 does not say "with" beauty but in beauty. Holiness has a form, a shape, a grammar. The Westminster Confession of Faith, 21.1, teaches that God may not be worshipped "according to the imaginations and devices of men." The regulative principle of worship is not about being old-fashioned. It is about being God-directed. When Macron and the Archbishop decide what Notre-Dame looks like without reference to the cathedral's own theological grammar, they are not being modern. They are being arbitrary.

The 340,000 signatories are not Luddites. They are witnesses to a truth their theology may not fully articulate but their instincts recognize: sacred space is not a blank canvas. Every arch, every vault, every window is a statement about what the people who built this cathedral believed. To replace those statements with the artistic preferences of this year's cultural establishment is to declare that the dead have no vote and the living have no debt to them.

See to it that no one takes you captive

Paul's warning in Colossians 2:8 names a specific danger: being taken captive "through philosophy and empty deceit, according to the tradition of men." The NLM framework identifies this as the threat behind "contextualized" and "contemporary" branding — the substitution of human artistic imagination for the grammar of the faith. Notre-Dame's windows are not being replaced because they are broken. They are being replaced because they are old. "Old" in this equation is not an aesthetic judgment. It is a verdict: the past has no authority over the present.

John Calvin stripped Geneva's churches of images but filled them with psalms because he understood that form teaches. He did not hate beauty. He hated beauty that lied. The question about Notre-Dame's windows is not whether they are 19th-century. It is whether they are true. The Reformers would not protest because something is old. They would protest because something is being replaced by "the imaginations of men rather than the grammar of the faith."

A spiritual temple, not a physical museum

Christ declared that "on this rock I will build My church" (Matthew 16:18) — not "on this cathedral." The true church is a spiritual temple, a people gathered across nations, tribes, and tongues. Notre-Dame is not the church. It is a building the church built. The distinction matters because it tells us what is at stake. The windows are not essential to salvation. But they are not nothing either. They are a witness. And replacing a witness with a trend is not neutral. It is an argument against the authority of the cloud of witnesses who came before.

The cathedral does not belong to Macron. It does not belong to the Archbishop. It does not belong to the 21st century. It belongs to the church across time — the church that built it, preserved it, and will outlive every president and every artist currently fashionable. Holiness is not updated.

The cathedral does not need to be contemporary to be relevant. It needs to be faithful to be true. Holiness is not updated.
