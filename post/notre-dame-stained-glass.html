340,000 people are praying over glass.

Notre-Dame wants to replace 19th-century stained glass with contemporary art. 340,000 people signed a petition. The Reformers would understand why.

Ecclesiology Psalm 27:4 Psalm 96:9

Notre-Dame de Paris is arguing with itself. President Emmanuel Macron and Archbishop Laurent Ulrich want to replace six 19th-century stained-glass windows by Eugène Viollet-le-Duc with contemporary creations by artist Claire Tabouret. Heritage group Sites et Monuments has filed an urgent legal appeal. Over 340,000 people have signed a petition to preserve the historic glass. Father Michel Viot called for peaceful on-site protests with rosaries, calling the plan an "attack on beauty." France's National Commission for Heritage and Architecture issued a negative opinion. The dispute is not about restoration. It is about who decides what sacred space looks like.

The argument is theological whether the participants know it or not. Viollet-le-Duc's windows are not mere decoration. They are catechesis in glass — biblical scenes arranged to teach, to narrate, to form the viewer in the grammar of salvation. Replacing them with contemporary art is not renovation. It is a statement about whether tradition has authority. The question is not old vs. new. The question is who decides.

David wrote: Psalm 27:4. One thing have I asked of the Lord — to gaze upon the beauty of the Lord. The Hebrew word for beauty is no'am, from a root meaning "pleasantness, delight, agreeableness." It is the same root used for the beauty of the Messiah in Isaiah 53:2. Beauty is not optional in biblical religion. It is a form of knowledge. The Reformers, who destroyed idolatrous images but filled their churches with the Psalms, understood this: beauty is argument. It persuades before the sermon begins.

The psalmist commands it: Psalm 96:9. Worship the Lord in the beauty of holiness. Not "with" beauty. In beauty. Holiness has a form. It has a shape. It has a grammar. The Westminster Confession of Faith, 21.1, teaches that God may not be worshipped "according to the imaginations and devices of men." The regulative principle of worship is not about being old-fashioned. It is about being God-directed. When Macron and the Archbishop decide what Notre-Dame looks like without reference to the cathedral's own grammar, they are not being modern. They are being arbitrary.

Notre-Dame de Paris, July 2013. CC BY-SA via Wikimedia Commons.

Worship the LORD in the beauty of holiness; let the whole earth stand in awe of him.

Psalm 96:9

The 340,000 people signing the petition are not Luddites. They are witnesses to a truth their theology may not fully articulate but their instincts recognize: sacred space is not a blank canvas. It is an argument. Every arch, every vault, every window is a statement about what the people who built it believed. Viollet-le-Duc's windows say: the gospel is a story that unfolds, that has shape, that is worth teaching in color and light. Tabouret's windows would say: the present moment is the final authority. These are not equivalent statements. They are competing theologies.

John Calvin, who stripped Geneva's churches of images but filled them with the Psalms sung in four-part harmony, understood the power of form. He did not hate beauty. He hated beauty that lied. The question about Notre-Dame's windows is not whether they are old. It is whether they are true. The Reformers would not protest because something is 19th-century. They would protest because something is being replaced by the imaginations of men rather than the grammar of the faith.

Father Viot called it an attack on beauty. He is right. But it is more than that. It is an attack on authority. The cathedral does not belong to Macron. It does not belong to the Archbishop. It does not belong to the 21st century. It belongs to the church — the church across time, the church that built it, the church that preserved it, the church that will outlive every president and every artist currently fashionable. The Psalmist says: worship the Lord in the beauty of holiness. Not in the beauty of novelty. Not in the beauty of relevance. In the beauty of holiness. And holiness is not updated.

The cathedral does not need to be contemporary to be relevant. It needs to be faithful to be true. Holiness is not updated.
