For the first time, young men are more religious than young women.

Gallup recorded the reversal this week. Jonathan Edwards would ask: what kind of religion?

Demographics 2 Corinthians 13:5 Edwards

Gallup published the numbers this week: for the first time in the polling firm's history, young American men are now more religious than young American women. In 2024–2025, 42% of men ages 18–29 said religion is "very important" to them. For women in the same bracket: roughly 30%. The gap has inverted. The pews are filling with young men.

Jonathan Edwards would not celebrate. He would ask: what kind of religion? In 1746, Edwards published A Treatise Concerning Religious Affections — the most rigorous attempt in Protestant history to distinguish true Christian conversion from every imitation that looks like it. Edwards was not interested in whether people said religion was "very important." He was interested in whether the Holy Spirit had actually regenerated their hearts. The two are not the same.

Edwards lists what he calls "signs" of truly gracious affection. Not all religious feeling is genuine. Some is natural — the product of temperament, culture, or social pressure. Some is spiritual pride — the devil's counterfeit, dressed in Christian language. Edwards' first test: does the person's love to God arise from God's own excellence, or from what God does for them? If a young man's religion is primarily about identity, community, or cultural belonging, his "religious affection" is natural, not gracious. It is real feeling. It is not saving faith.

Examine yourselves, to see whether you are in the faith. Test yourselves. Or do you not realize this about yourselves, that Jesus Christ is in you? — unless indeed you fail to meet the test!

2 Corinthians 13:5

For the reader who does not know the Bible: Paul is writing to a church he founded, and he commands them to test themselves. Self-examination is not doubt. It is a spiritual discipline commanded by an apostle. The modern church often treats questioning one's own salvation as a lack of faith. Paul treats it as obedience. Matthew 7:21–23 records Jesus warning that some who call Him "Lord" with full confidence will hear Him reply: "I never knew you." Religious activity is not the same as knowing Christ. That distinction is the heartbeat of Reformed theology.

Edwards' second test: love to holiness. The person who has truly been regenerated does not merely love the idea of God. They love holiness — the actual moral character of God, including His hatred of sin. Edwards writes: "A true love to God must begin with a delight in his holiness." If a young man's religion makes him feel righteous without making him feel the weight of his own sin, Edwards would classify that affection as counterfeit. The mark of genuine conversion is not confidence. It is humility.

Edwards describes the spiritually proud person as one who is already "full of light" — confident in his own spiritual state, feeling good about how he is doing. Such a person, Edwards warns, is not merely full of light. He is full of himself. The trap of religious revival — whether the Great Awakening of 1740 or any modern movement — is that enthusiasm can inflate the ego rather than crucify it. Edwards watched this happen in his own congregation. People wept, trembled, and spoke in fervent language. Some were genuinely converted. Others were merely excited. The difference was invisible to the observer. It was visible to God.

Jeremiah 17:9–10 says the heart is "deceitful above all things, and desperately sick." For the reader who does not know the Bible: this is not a poetic metaphor. It is a diagnostic claim about human nature. Your own heart can lie to you. It can dress political identity in religious language. It can dress cultural resentment in moral conviction. It can dress loneliness in spiritual hunger. And because the heart is deceitful, self-examination is not optional. It is survival.

The Reformers did not measure the health of the church by attendance. They measured it by fidelity. John Calvin, writing in the Institutes (3.2), warns against "false confidence" — the human tendency to assume that outward participation in religious acts guarantees inward acceptance by God. The Heidelberg Catechism (Q.21) asks: "What is true faith?" The answer: "True faith is not only a certain knowledge... but also a hearty trust." Trust is the key word. Not affirmation. Not identification. Not enthusiasm. Trust — in a Savior whom you know you do not deserve.

So what does the church do with this demographic shift? Not celebrate. Not panic. Preach. The gospel does not need the culture to produce more religious young men. It needs the culture to produce regenerated young men — and women, and old men, and children. The Reformers taught that the Holy Spirit blows where He wishes. You cannot demographic-engineer revival. You can only preach the Word, administer the sacraments, and pray that the Spirit gives life where the statistics cannot.

The young man walking into church because he wants identity, structure, or community is not the enemy. He is the mission field. The church's job is not to ask "Why are you here?" The church's job is to preach Christ crucified — and let the Spirit sort the wheat from the tares. Edwards spent his life trying to tell the difference. The modern church should not stop trying. But it should also remember: Edwards' final test was not doctrinal knowledge. It was love. Love to God. Love to holiness. Love to the brethren. If that is present, the demographic trend is God's business. If it is absent, the demographic trend is a warning.

Edwards' final test was not doctrinal knowledge. It was love. Love to God. Love to holiness. Love to the brethren.
