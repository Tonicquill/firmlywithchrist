The U.S. Census Bureau reported it in January: 40% of all US births in 2023 were to unmarried women. Among women over 30, the rate is climbing fastest. Cohabitation now accounts for more than a third of "unmarried" births. The old categories — married, single, divorced — no longer describe how people actually live.

The temptation is to moralize. The Reformers did not moralize. They theologized. They saw marriage not as a social convention but as a covenant. Malachi 2:14–15: "She is your companion and your wife by covenant. Did he not make them one, with a portion of the Spirit in their union? And what was the one God seeking? Godly offspring." The Hebrew word berith means a bond witnessed by God Himself, creating obligations that exist regardless of the feelings of the people in it.

For the reader who does not know the Bible: the text says God's purpose for marriage is mutual help first, children second. He calls Himself the witness — the legal guarantor — of a bond that exists for the good of the people in it. The Hebrew word translated "companion" implies a partnership of commitment, not merely attraction. When marriage is treated as a feeling rather than a covenant, the structure that children depend on becomes conditional on adult affection. The text does not blame individuals for structural collapse. It names the collapse itself: a covenant was not kept — by death, by abandonment, by sin, or sometimes because a covenant was never made.

The Westminster Confession calls marriage WCF 24.2. Notice the order: mutual help first, children second. The covenant serves the people in it, not the other way around. A single parent is not a failed covenant. They are someone doing two jobs because a covenant was not kept — by death, by abandonment, by sin, or sometimes because a covenant was never made.

That said, the choice to bring a child into the world outside covenant marriage is itself a sin against God's design. Not because sex is shameful, but because the child is made to bear the cost of adult autonomy. The Reformers called this defrauding — taking what God designed for mutual obligation and using it for self-fulfillment. The child is not the sin. The child is the image-bearer who suffers the sin. And the parent is not beyond redemption. They are a sinner for whom Christ died, now doing the hard work of provision in a structure they did not build. The church must say both things: the choice was wrong, and the child is precious. One does not cancel the other.

Deuteronomy 23:2 once excluded from Israel's assembly anyone born outside the marriage covenant. The New Testament revises this. Galatians 3:28: "There is neither Jew nor Greek, there is neither slave nor free, there is neither male nor female; for you are all one in Christ Jesus." The old covenant marked illegitimacy with exclusion. The new covenant marks every adopted child of God with inclusion. The child's standing before God is not determined by the parents' sin. It is determined by Christ's righteousness.

For the reader who does not know the Bible: this is a radical reversal. Under the old system, your parents' moral failures could bar you from the community. Under the new system, your standing depends on Christ's moral perfection, not your parents' choices. The child of an unmarried mother is not second-class. The child of an unmarried mother who trusts Christ is a full heir of God, with the same inheritance as the child of a pastor.

Here is where the Reformed tradition becomes militantly inconvenient for both sides of the culture war. The secularist attack says the church only cares about "family values" when it is shaming single mothers. The Bible's reply is not to soften the covenant. It is to redirect the wrath. Exodus 22:22–24: "You shall not mistreat any widow or fatherless child. If you do mistreat them, and they cry out to me, I will surely hear their cry, and my wrath will burn." The Hebrew verb for "mistreat" (anah) means to afflict, to humiliate, to treat as less than human. God's specified target is not the woman who had a child without a husband. It is the person who makes her life harder once she has.

For the reader who does not know the Bible: this is not a gentle suggestion. It is a threat. The text says God personally guarantees vengeance against anyone who afflicts a fatherless child or a widow. The covenant is not defended by making outsiders miserable. It is defended by making their protection non-negotiable.

Give justice to the weak and the fatherless; maintain the right of the afflicted and the destitute. Rescue the weak and the needy; deliver them from the hand of the wicked.

Psalm 82:3–4

James 1:27 defines pure religion in two clauses: "to visit orphans and widows in their affliction, and to keep oneself unstained from the world." The order matters. The first half is active care for the vulnerable. The second half is personal holiness. The text does not separate them. It binds them. A church that speaks of covenant collapse without concrete provision for the children caught in it is not preserving doctrine. It is preserving posture.

The historical precedent is specific. George Müller, a 19th-century German Reformed minister in Bristol, England, built five orphan houses that cared for over 10,000 children. He did not run a social program. He ran a demonstration. Müller believed that if God had commanded care for the fatherless, then God would fund it — and he refused to ask anyone for money, relying entirely on prayer and anonymous gifts. The result: the homes ran for decades, debt-free, feeding thousands daily. Müller's orphanages were not a departure from Reformed doctrine. They were an application of it. He took Exodus 22 literally. So did the people who gave.

James 2:1 adds a further constraint: "Show no partiality as you hold the faith in our Lord Jesus Christ." The Greek word for "partiality" (prosopolempsia) refers to the ancient practice of judging by face-value — wealth, status, family completeness. A church that treats two-parent families as first-class and single-parent families as second-class is not preserving morality. It is violating the text it claims to defend.

The Westminster Shorter Catechism asks what the eighth commandment requires. The answer: "The eighth commandment requireth the lawful procuring and furthering the wealth and outward estate of ourselves and others" (WSC Q.74). The Reformers called this the duty of love — not charity as sentiment, but charity as obligation. The single mother is not a project. She is a neighbor. The fatherless child is not a statistic. He is the specific target of God's personal protection.

So what does the church do? Not vague "support." Concrete obligation. Here is the Reformed prescription: food provision for families in immediate need, not as program but as duty; childcare coverage so single parents can work; direct financial assistance without means-testing humiliation; men held accountable for abandonment where traceable, not by shame but by restoration; adoption and fostering treated as spiritual discipline, not niche ministry; and the Lord's Supper offered without interrogation about household structure. The covenant is rebuilt not by making outsiders feel the gap, but by closing it.
